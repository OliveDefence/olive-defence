English Grammar · Adjectives

Adjectives —
Degrees, Order & Usage

Adjectives are tested in CDS, NDA and AFCAT across all three question types — Spotting Errors, Fill in the Blanks, and Sentence Improvement. The traps include wrong degrees of comparison, double comparatives, absolute adjectives, incorrect order in a noun phrase, and confused pairs like elder/older and farther/further. This module covers every pattern.

Comprehensive CDS · NDA · AFCAT 20 Practice Questions

An adjective modifies a noun or pronoun — it describes, identifies, or quantifies it. In competitive exams, adjectives are tested mainly on: (1) Degrees of comparison — forming and using positive, comparative, and superlative correctly; (2) Confused pairs — elder/older, farther/further, later/latter, latest/last; (3) Absolute adjectives — perfect, unique, dead, complete, round, which cannot be compared; (4) Order of adjectives in a noun phrase; and (5) Adjective vs adverb confusion.

Section 1 — Degrees of Comparison
📐
Forming Degrees — Rules for -er / -est vs More / Most

⚑ Double Comparative / Double Superlative — Always Wrong
  • ✗ "more taller", "more bigger", "more happier" — never add "more" to an -er form
  • ✗ "most tallest", "most bravest" — never add "most" to an -est form
  • ✓ "taller", "more careful", "most intelligent"
  • This is the most tested adjective error in all three exams
The "Than" Rule for Comparatives

A comparative adjective must always be followed by "than" when making a comparison — never "from" or "to".
✓ "He is taller than his brother."  |  ✗ "He is taller from his brother."
Exception: "different from" (not "different than" in formal British English used in these exams).

⚖️
Using Degrees Correctly — Structures & Rules
As…as, not so…as, the…the, -er than any other — fixed patterns
▾
StructureRuleExample
as + positive + asPositive degree — equal comparisonHe is as tall as his captain. ✓
not so + positive + asInequality — less thanHe is not so tall as his captain. ✓
comparative + than any otherSuperlative meaning via comparativeHe is braver than any other soldier. ✓
one of the + superlative + plural nounSuperlative with plural nounHe is one of the bravest soldiers. ✓
the + comparative, the + comparativeProportional increaseThe higher you climb, the colder it gets. ✓
no other + singular noun + positiveSuperlative meaning via positiveNo other soldier is as brave as he. ✓
⚑ "One of the + superlative" — Noun Must Be Plural
  • ✗ "He is one of the bravest soldier." → ✓ "one of the bravest soldiers"
  • ✗ "She is one of the best student." → ✓ "one of the best students"
  • After "one of the + superlative", the noun must always be plural
Illogical Comparisons — Comparing Unlike Things
  • ✗ "The climate of Mumbai is better than Delhi." → comparing climate to a city
  • ✓ "The climate of Mumbai is better than that of Delhi." ✓
  • ✗ "India's army is larger than China." → ✓ "India's army is larger than that of China." ✓
Section 2 — Absolute Adjectives
🚫
Adjectives That Cannot Be Compared
Perfect, unique, dead, infinite, round, complete — no degrees possible
▾

Absolute adjectives represent qualities that are all-or-nothing — they cannot exist in degrees. Something is either unique or it is not; something is either perfect or it is not. Using "more", "most", "very", or "-er/-est" with these is grammatically incorrect.

Common Absolute Adjectives — Never Compare These
  • unique, absolute, universal, infinite, eternal, immortal, omnipotent
  • perfect, ideal, complete, total, entire, whole, full
  • dead, alive, pregnant, blind, deaf, dumb (states that are either present or not)
  • round, square, circular, triangular, oval (geometric absolutes)
  • impossible, inevitable, certain, correct, wrong, true, false
Errors with Absolute Adjectives
  • ✗ "This plan is more perfect." → ✓ "This plan is perfect." (or "more nearly perfect")
  • ✗ "The shape is very round." → ✓ "The shape is round."
  • ✗ "It is very impossible." → ✓ "It is impossible."
  • ✗ "He found a more unique solution." → ✓ "He found a unique solution."
Acceptable Modifiers for Absolute Adjectives
  • "nearly perfect", "almost complete", "virtually impossible" — these are acceptable because "nearly/almost" shows approach to the absolute
  • "absolutely unique", "completely dead" — the adverb reinforces, not grades, the absolute
Section 3 — Commonly Confused Adjective Pairs
👥
Elder / Older  ·  Latest / Last  ·  Nearest / Next
Context determines the correct choice — all tested in spotting errors
▾
PairUsageCorrect Example
ElderSeniority within a family (brother, sister, son, daughter) — not followed by "than"He is my elder brother. ✓
OlderAge comparison between any two persons/things — followed by "than"He is older than his friend. ✓
LatestMost recent (time-based) — still ongoing possibilityHave you read the latest news? ✓
LastFinal in a series — nothing comes after itThis is the last chapter of the book. ✓
LaterMore recent time (time-based comparative)We will discuss this at a later date. ✓
LatterThe second of two previously mentioned itemsI prefer the latter option. ✓ (of two)
NearestClosest in distanceWhat is the nearest hospital? ✓
NextImmediately following in order/sequenceTake the next left turn. ✓

⚑ Elder — Two Key Restrictions
  • "Elder" cannot be used with "than": ✗ "He is elder than me." → ✓ "He is older than me."
  • "Elder" cannot be used predicatively (after a verb): ✗ "He is elder." → ✓ "He is the elder." (with article) or "He is older."
  • "Elder" is only used attributively (before a noun) in family contexts: "my elder brother", "her elder sister"

  • "Between strategy and execution, the former must be sound." (strategy = 1st) ✓

  • ✗ "the asleep guard" → ✓ "the sleeping guard" (use participial adj)
  • ✗ "an afraid recruit" → ✓ "a frightened recruit"
  • ✗ "an alive prisoner" → ✓ "a living prisoner"
  • ✗ "He is utter wrong." → ✓ "He is utterly wrong." (utter = attributive adj; utterly = adverb)

⚑ "Superior/Inferior/Senior/Junior/Prior" — Always "to", Never "than"
  • These five adjectives are already comparative in meaning — they should never be followed by "than"
  • ✗ "He is senior than me." → ✓ "He is senior to me."
  • ✗ "This model is superior than the old one." → ✓ "…superior to the old one."
  • Also: never use "more superior" — they are already comparative; no double comparison

Latin-Origin Comparatives — Followed by "to", Never "than"
These 12 adjectives are already comparative — use "to" not "than"
  • senior to, junior to — rank/age hierarchy
  • superior to, inferior to — quality hierarchy
  • prior to, posterior to — time sequence (prior = before; posterior = after)
  • major, minor — relative importance (often used alone; "major to" is rare)
  • exterior to, interior to, anterior to, ulterior to — position/motivation
  • prefer…to — (prefer is a verb but follow same rule): "I prefer tea to coffee." ✓ not "prefer tea than coffee"
Adjectives — Complete Quick Reference
Forming Degrees — Key Rules
  • 1 syllable → -er / -est (tall → taller → tallest)
  • CVC → double last consonant (big → bigger)
  • 2-syll ending -y → change y to i (happy → happier)
  • 3+ syllables → more / most
  • ✗ Never "more taller" / "most tallest" — double comparison
  • Comparative always followed by "than" — never "from" or "to"
Critical Irregular Pairs
  • good → better → best | bad → worse → worst
  • much/many → more → most | little → less → least
  • far: farther/farthest (distance) | further/furthest (additional)
  • old: older/oldest (general) | elder/eldest (family seniority)
  • late: later/latest (time) | latter/last (order/sequence)
  • near: nearer/nearest (distance) | next (sequence)
  • fore → former → foremost
Absolute Adjectives — Never Compare
  • unique, perfect, ideal, absolute, complete, total, entire
  • dead, alive, pregnant, blind, deaf, infinite, eternal
  • round, square, circular, correct, impossible, inevitable
  • ✗ "more unique", "very perfect", "most complete" — all wrong
  • ✓ "nearly perfect", "almost complete" — acceptable
Latin Comparatives → "to" not "than"
  • senior to, junior to (never "senior than")
  • superior to, inferior to (never "superior than")
  • prior to, anterior to, posterior to
  • Also: prefer…to (verb but same rule)
  • Never "more superior" — already comparative in meaning
Adjectives with Fixed Prepositions
  • different from (not than) | similar to (not with)
  • worthy of | proud of | innocent of
  • good at (skill) / good for (benefit)
  • anxious about (worry) / anxious for (eager)
  • responsible for | capable of | eligible for
  • familiar with | blind to | deaf to
Order of Adjectives (OSASCOM)
  • Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Colour → Origin → Material → Purpose
  • a beautiful large old round blue French silver decorative vase
  • Attributive only: elder, former, latter, inner, outer, utter, mere, chief
  • Predicative only: afraid, alive, asleep, awake, aware, ashamed, ablaze, aloof
  • "One of the + superlative" → noun must be plural
  • Illogical comparison → add "that of" for singular nouns
