E14 — Verbs
Types · Principal Parts · Transitive & Intransitive · Subject-Verb Agreement · Modals & Helping Verbs · Tense Errors · Exam Traps — CDS · NDA · AFCAT
CDS NDA AFCAT 7 Sections 15 Questions

A verb is the engine of every sentence — it expresses an action, occurrence, or state of being. In CDS/NDA/AFCAT English, verbs are tested across error spotting, sentence improvement, fill-in-the-blank, and comprehension. The highest-yield areas are: subject-verb agreement, modal auxiliary usage, tense consistency, transitive/intransitive errors, infinitive vs gerund, and irregular verb forms.

Section 1 — Types of Verbs
🗂️
Main Classification
Main · Auxiliary · Modal · Transitive · Intransitive · Linking
▾
TypeDefinitionExamples
Main (Lexical) VerbCarries the primary meaning of the sentencemarch, command, fight, train, deploy, report
Auxiliary (Helping) VerbHelps the main verb form tense, mood, or voicebe, have, do (primary auxiliaries)
Modal AuxiliaryExpresses possibility, ability, obligation, permissioncan, could, may, might, shall, should, will, would, must, ought to, used to, need, dare
Transitive VerbRequires a direct object to complete its meaningThe officer ordered a retreat. / She leads the squad.
Intransitive VerbDoes not take a direct object; complete in itselfThe troops marched. / He arrived.
Linking (Copular) VerbConnects subject to a predicate noun or adjectivebe, seem, appear, become, feel, look, smell, taste, sound, remain, grow, turn
Ditransitive VerbTakes both a direct and indirect objectHe gave the platoon orders. (IO + DO)
⚑ Verbs That Are Both Transitive and Intransitive
  • run: "He runs fast." (intransitive) / "He runs the unit." (transitive)
  • move: "The convoy moved." (intr.) / "He moved the troops." (tr.)
  • fly: "The pilot flew." (intr.) / "He flew the aircraft." (tr.)
  • Context determines which — error when an intransitive verb is given an object or a transitive verb is left without one.
📐
Finite vs Non-Finite Verbs
Infinitive · Gerund · Participle — the three non-finite forms
▾

A finite verb is limited by person, number, and tense — it can stand as the predicate of a sentence. A non-finite verb is not limited — it cannot alone be the sentence predicate.

Non-Finite FormStructureFunctionExample
Infinitiveto + base formNoun, adjective, or adverb function"To lead is an honour." / "He trained to compete."
Gerundbase + -ing (noun use)Acts as a noun (subject, object)"Training is essential." / "He enjoys commanding."
Present Participlebase + -ing (adjective/adverb)Modifies nouns or verbs"The marching soldiers." / "Hearing the order, he moved."
Past ParticipleV3 formUsed in perfect tenses and passive voice"The mission was accomplished."
⚑ Gerund vs Infinitive — The Key Exam Trap
  • Verbs followed by GERUND only: enjoy, avoid, admit, consider, deny, finish, imagine, keep, mind, miss, postpone, practise, risk, suggest, recommend
    → "He enjoys commanding." (not "to command") ✓
  • Verbs followed by INFINITIVE only: agree, arrange, choose, decide, expect, fail, hope, learn, manage, offer, plan, prepare, promise, refuse, want, wish
    → "He decided to withdraw." (not "withdrawing") ✓
  • Verbs followed by EITHER (meaning changes):
    — remember: "I remember locking the armoury." (did it) vs "I remembered to lock it." (must do it)
    — forget: "I forgot meeting him." (past event) vs "I forgot to meet him." (task undone)
    — stop: "He stopped smoking." (quit) vs "He stopped to smoke." (paused in order to)
    — try: "He tried opening the safe." (attempted) vs "He tried to open it." (made effort)
Section 2 — Subject-Verb Agreement
⚖️
Core Agreement Rules
Either/Neither · Each/Every · Intervening phrases · Inversion · Collective nouns
▾
The 12 Core S-V Agreement Rules
  • Either/Neither as subjects → singular verb: "Neither option is viable." ✓
  • Either…or / Neither…nor → verb agrees with the nearer subject: "Neither the colonel nor the majors were present." ✓
  • Each / Every / Everyone / Everything / Anybody / Nobody → singular verb: "Each cadet has submitted." ✓
  • Intervening phrase (with, along with, as well as, in addition to, together with, accompanied by) → verb agrees with the grammatical subject before the phrase: "The CO, along with his officers, was briefed." ✓
  • None → traditionally singular ("None of the soldiers was injured"), but plural is now widely accepted when referring to a group ("None of the soldiers were injured").
  • A number of → plural verb; The number of → singular verb: "A number of cadets have passed." / "The number of vacancies is high." ✓
  • More than one → singular verb: "More than one officer was present." ✓
  • One of → singular: "One of the soldiers has been selected." ✓
  • Many a / Many an → singular verb: "Many a soldier has sacrificed his life." ✓
  • As well as / Together with / With → verb agrees with first subject: "The General, as well as his staff, is attending." ✓
  • Relative pronoun "who/which/that" → verb agrees with the antecedent: "He is one of those officers who lead by example." ✓ (antecedent = officers)
  • Titles, subjects as names, amounts → singular: "Three kilometres is a short march." / "Physics is mandatory." ✓
Most Tested S-V Agreement Errors
  • ✗ "Each of the officers have reported." → ✓ "Each of the officers has reported."
  • ✗ "The team, along with supporters, were briefed." → ✓ "…was briefed." (team = subject)
  • ✗ "Neither the map nor the compass were useful." → ✓ "…was useful." (nearer subject: compass = singular)
  • ✗ "A number of mistakes has been made." → ✓ "…have been made."
  • ✗ "Many a cadet have graduated." → ✓ "Many a cadet has graduated."
Section 3 — Auxiliary (Helping) Verbs
🔧
Primary Auxiliaries — Be, Have, Do
Forming tenses, passive voice, questions & negations
▾

The three primary auxiliaries are special because they can also function as main verbs. As auxiliaries, they help form tenses, voice, questions, and negations.

AuxiliaryFormsFunctionsExample
BEam, is, are, was, were, been, beingProgressive tenses; passive voice"He is training." / "Orders were issued."
HAVEhave, has, had, havingPerfect tenses"She has completed the course." / "He had arrived."
DOdo, does, didQuestions; negations; emphasis"Does he qualify?" / "He did report on time." (emphatic)
Tense Formation with Primary Auxiliaries
  • Present Progressive: am/is/are + V-ing → "He is marching."
  • Past Progressive: was/were + V-ing → "They were advancing."
  • Present Perfect: have/has + V3 → "She has completed training."
  • Past Perfect: had + V3 → "He had submitted the report."
  • Passive Voice: be + V3 → "The base was secured."
  • Perfect Progressive: have/has/had + been + V-ing → "He has been training for months."
⚑ "Do" — Never Used With Other Auxiliaries
  • ✗ "Did he went?" → ✓ "Did he go?" (do/does/did → base form of main verb)
  • ✗ "He does can swim." → ✓ "He can swim." (do not double-auxiliary)
  • ✗ "She didn't went." → ✓ "She didn't go."
Section 4 — Modal Auxiliaries
🎯
Modal Rules — The Non-Negotiables
No -s in third person · Always followed by bare infinitive · No two modals together
▾
Three Absolute Modal Rules

1. Modals do not change for person or number — never add -s/-es in third person singular.
✗ "He cans do it." → ✓ "He can do it."

2. Modals are always followed by the bare infinitive (base form, no "to" — except "ought to", "used to", "have to", "need to").
✗ "She must to report." → ✓ "She must report."

3. Two modals cannot be used together in standard English.
✗ "He will can do it." → ✓ "He will be able to do it."

Can vs Could — Ability & Possibility
  • can = present ability: "He can run 10 km." ✓
  • could = past ability: "He could run 10 km before the injury." ✓
  • could = polite request / possibility: "Could you hand me the report?" ✓
  • can = informal permission: "You can leave now." ✓ (formal: may)
  • could have + V3 = unrealised past possibility: "He could have been selected." ✓
  • ✗ "He can have won yesterday." → ✓ "He could have won yesterday." (past context)
May vs Might — Permission & Possibility
  • may = formal permission: "You may proceed." ✓
  • may = present/future possibility (stronger): "It may rain." ✓ (~50% chance)
  • might = past possibility OR weaker current possibility: "It might rain." ✓ (~25% chance)
  • might have + V3 = unrealised past: "He might have survived if treated." ✓
  • ✗ "He said he may come yesterday." → ✓ "He said he might come." (reported speech)
Shall vs Will — Volition, Promises & Determination
  • Traditional rule: shall with I/we (simple future); will with he/she/it/you/they
  • For determination / promise / threat: reverse — I/we + will; he/she/they + shall
  • "I will not surrender." (determination — speaker's strong will) ✓
  • "He shall be punished." (authority's decree — determination for another person) ✓
  • In modern usage, "will" is used for all persons; "shall" is largely formal/legal.
  • Should = obligation / advice / condition: "You should report immediately." ✓
  • Would = past habit / polite request / conditional: "He would patrol every night." ✓ (past habit)
Must vs Have To vs Ought To vs Should
  • must = strong internal obligation / logical deduction (present/future): "He must obey orders." ✓
  • must not = prohibition: "You must not enter." ✓ (strong — not "don't have to")
  • don't have to / needn't = absence of obligation (NOT prohibition): "You don't have to attend." ✓
  • have to = external obligation: "He has to submit by 1700 hrs." ✓
  • ought to = moral duty / mild obligation (= should, slightly stronger): "Officers ought to lead from the front." ✓
  • should = advice / expectation: "You should prepare for CDS." ✓
  • must have + V3 = logical deduction about past: "He must have left early." ✓
⚑ Must Not vs Need Not / Don't Have To
  • must not = you are PROHIBITED from doing it (strong negative command)
  • need not / don't have to = there is no obligation, but you may if you wish
  • "You must not enter the restricted zone." (prohibited — if you do, it's wrong)
  • "You need not salute in casual dress." (not required — but not forbidden)
  • ✗ Confusing these two completely reverses the meaning — a top exam error trap.
Used To vs Would — Past Habits
  • used to = past habit OR past state that NO LONGER exists: "He used to patrol this route." (habit) / "There used to be a camp here." (state) ✓
  • would = past repeated action ONLY (not states): "He would patrol every night." ✓ — but NOT "There would be a camp here." ✗ for states
  • ✗ "He would live in Delhi before joining." → ✓ "He used to live in Delhi…" (state, not action)
  • be used to + gerund = be accustomed to (present habit): "He is used to marching long distances." ✓
  • get used to + gerund = become accustomed to: "He got used to the altitude." ✓
Need & Dare — Semi-Modals
  • need as modal (negative/question): "He need not report." / "Need he report?" — no -s, followed by bare infinitive ✓
  • need as main verb: "He needs to report." / "He doesn't need to report." — inflects normally ✓
  • dare as modal: "He dare not disobey." ✓
  • dare as main verb: "He doesn't dare to disobey." ✓
  • ✗ "He needs not report." → ✓ "He need not report." (modal use — no -s)
Section 5 — Tense & Time Errors
⏱️
Sequence of Tenses
Main clause past → subordinate clause past · Reported speech · Stative verbs
▾

When the main clause verb is in a past tense, the subordinate clause verb must also be in a past form — this is the rule of sequence of tenses.

Sequence of Tenses — Correct
  • He said he was ready. (not "is") ✓
  • She asked whether he had submitted the report. ✓
  • The CO ordered that the troops should advance. ✓
  • They believed the enemy had retreated. ✓
Exceptions to Sequence of Tenses

1. Universal / habitual truths → always present tense even after past main verb:
"He said that the Earth revolves around the Sun." ✓
"The teacher explained that water boils at 100°C." ✓

2. Historical facts → may remain in past even in reported speech:
"He said that the Battle of Panipat was fought in 1526." ✓

3. Habitual actions still true → present allowed:
"He told me he goes to the gym every day." ✓ (still true)

⚑ Stative Verbs — Never Use in Progressive Tense
  • Stative verbs describe states, not actions, and do NOT take -ing forms normally
  • Perception: see, hear, smell, taste, feel (in stative sense)
  • Emotion/desire: love, hate, want, wish, prefer, like, dislike
  • Cognition: know, believe, understand, think (opinion), remember, forget, mean, realise
  • Possession: have (=possess), own, belong, contain, consist
  • ✗ "I am knowing the answer." → ✓ "I know the answer."
  • ✗ "She is wanting to become an officer." → ✓ "She wants to become an officer."
  • ✗ "He is having a car." → ✓ "He has a car." (have = possess)
Section 6 — Active & Passive Voice
🔄
Voice — Active to Passive Conversion
Structure · Tense-wise patterns · Verbs that cannot be made passive
▾
Passive Voice Formula

Object of active → Subject of passive
Active verb → appropriate form of be + V3
Subject of active → by + agent (optional, often omitted)

TenseActivePassive
Simple PresentHe commands the unit.The unit is commanded by him.
Simple PastShe led the patrol.The patrol was led by her.
Present PerfectThey have secured the base.The base has been secured.
Past PerfectHe had completed training.Training had been completed by him.
Future SimpleThey will deploy the squad.The squad will be deployed.
ModalThey must verify the order.The order must be verified.
⚑ Intransitive Verbs Cannot Be Made Passive
  • Intransitive verbs have no object — without an object, there is nothing to become the passive subject.
  • ✗ "The accident was occurred on the highway." → ✓ "The accident occurred." (occur = intransitive)
  • ✗ "The soldier was died in battle." → ✓ "The soldier died in battle." (die = intransitive)
  • ✗ "The convoy was arrived at dawn." → ✓ "The convoy arrived at dawn."
Section 7 — Conditionals & Subjunctive
💬
Conditionals — Four Types
Zero · First · Second · Third — tense–modal pairings
▾
TypeIf-ClauseMain ClauseMeaning
ZeroIf + Simple PresentSimple PresentUniversal truth: "If you heat metal, it expands."
FirstIf + Simple Presentwill + baseReal/likely future: "If he trains hard, he will pass."
SecondIf + Simple Pastwould + baseUnreal present: "If he trained harder, he would pass."
ThirdIf + Past Perfectwould have + V3Unreal past: "If he had trained, he would have passed."
⚑ Never Use "Would" in the If-Clause
  • ✗ "If he would train harder, he would pass." → ✓ "If he trained harder, he would pass."
  • ✗ "If I would have known, I would have acted." → ✓ "If I had known, I would have acted."
  • Exception: "would" can appear in if-clauses only when it means "be willing to" — "If you would kindly follow me…"
Subjunctive Mood — "Were" in All Persons

The subjunctive is used for hypothetical, wishful, or contrary-to-fact situations. In formal English, use "were" for all persons (not "was"):

"If I were a general, I would reform the system." ✓ (not "was")
"I wish I were posted in Ladakh." ✓
"He behaved as if he were the commander." ✓
"It is essential that he be present." ✓ (subjunctive in "that" clause after demand/suggestion)

Quick Reference — Core Rules
Subject-Verb Agreement — Top Rules
  • Either/Neither (alone) → singular verb
  • Each/Every/Many a → singular verb
  • Either…or / Neither…nor → verb agrees with nearer subject
  • Along with / As well as / Together with → agree with 1st subject
  • A number of → plural; The number of → singular
  • More than one + singular noun → singular verb
Modal — Three Absolute Rules
  • Modals never add -s/-es in 3rd person singular
  • Modals followed by bare infinitive (except ought to, used to, have to, need to)
  • No two modals together → use alternative: "will be able to"
  • ✗ "must to go" → ✓ "must go"
  • ✗ "will can do" → ✓ "will be able to do"
Must Not vs Need Not
  • must not = PROHIBITION — you are forbidden
  • need not / don't have to = no obligation — optional
  • "You must not enter." (banned)
  • "You need not wait." (not required)
  • Swapping these reverses the entire meaning
Perfect Modals — Past Reference
  • should have + V3 = duty not done (regret)
  • could have + V3 = unrealised past ability
  • must have + V3 = logical deduction about past
  • would have + V3 = unreal past result (3rd conditional)
  • need not have + V3 = unnecessary past action
  • ✗ "should had" → ✓ "should have"
Stative Verbs — No Progressive Form
  • Know, understand, believe, think (opinion), mean, realise
  • Want, wish, prefer, like, love, hate, need
  • See, hear, smell, taste, feel (stative sense)
  • Have (= possess), own, belong, contain, consist
  • ✗ "am knowing / is wanting"
Conditionals — If-Clause Tense
  • Zero: If + present → present (universal truth)
  • First: If + present → will + base (likely)
  • Second: If + past → would + base (unreal present)
  • Third: If + past perfect → would have + V3 (unreal past)
  • ✗ "If he would train" → ✓ "If he trained"
Passive Voice — Cannot Be Passive
  • Intransitive verbs have no object → cannot be passive
  • occur, die, arrive, go, come, rise, fall, sleep, exist
  • ✗ "was occurred / was died / was arrived"
  • Passive = be + V3; confirm V3 not V2
  • ✗ "was gave" → ✓ "was given"
Key Confusable Verb Pairs
  • lie (recline): lay → lain (intransitive)
  • lay (place): laid → laid (transitive)
  • rise (go up): rose → risen (intransitive)
  • raise (lift): raised → raised (transitive)
  • hang (object): hung; hang (execute): hanged
  • used to for states; would for repeated actions only
