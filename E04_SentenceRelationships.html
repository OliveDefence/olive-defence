English · Critical Reasoning

Sentence
Relationships

Read two sentences and identify the precise logical relationship between them — does S2 confirm, contradict, illustrate, qualify, generalise, or question S1? A high-value topic in CDS, NDA and AFCAT that rewards systematic thinking over guesswork.

Intermediate Level CDS · NDA · AFCAT 20 Practice Questions

In sentence relationship questions you are given two sentences and must identify the logical role the second sentence plays relative to the first. The eight relationship types — Confirms, Contradicts, Illustrates, Qualifies, Generalises, Questions, Restates and Infers — each have distinct signal patterns. Master the 3-step method and the signal words for each type and you will rarely get these wrong.

Section 1 — The 3-Step Method
🧭
How to Approach Every Question
A reliable 3-step process that eliminates wrong answers fast
▾
The 3-Step Method

Step 1 — Understand S1 completely. Ask: What is the main claim, fact, or situation in Sentence 1? Label it mentally: is it a rule, a fact, a problem, a policy, an opinion?

Step 2 — Understand what S2 adds. Ask: Does S2 agree or disagree? Is it specific or general? Does it give a reason, an example, a counter-point, or a conclusion?

Step 3 — Match to a relationship type. Compare your S2 finding to the eight types. One will fit precisely — eliminate the rest using the "signal word" test.

⚑ The Most Common Mistake

Candidates often confuse Confirms and Illustrates. The difference: Confirms = S2 provides another fact/evidence that the claim in S1 is true. Illustrates = S2 gives a specific example of the general idea in S1. Ask yourself: "Is S2 an example or is S2 additional proof?"

Section 2 — The 8 Relationship Types
✅
Confirms
S2 supports or validates the claim made in S1
▾
✅ CONFIRMS — S2 backs up S1 with evidence or a parallel fact

S2 is consistent with S1 and adds supporting evidence, data, or a corroborating fact. Both sentences agree; S2 strengthens the claim in S1.

Example

S1: Regular physical exercise significantly improves mental health.

S2: Studies show that people who exercise daily report lower levels of anxiety and depression.

→ S2 is a research finding that confirms the claim in S1 with direct evidence.

Signal Patterns in S2
  • Statistics, data, research findings that agree with S1
  • Another event/fact that parallels the claim in S1
  • Words: "studies show…", "research confirms…", "data suggests…"
❌
Contradicts
S2 directly opposes or negates the claim in S1
▾
❌ CONTRADICTS — S2 disagrees with or disproves S1

S2 directly conflicts with S1. The two sentences cannot both be true at the same time in the same context. S2 may deny, disprove, or present facts that go against S1.

Example

S1: The new highway project will greatly reduce commute time for residents.

S2: Traffic surveys indicate that the highway will add 40 minutes to daily commutes due to bottlenecks at entry points.

→ S2 directly opposes the claim in S1 — it contradicts it.

Signal Patterns in S2
  • Opposite outcome to what S1 claims
  • Data or facts that are incompatible with S1
  • Words: "however…", "on the contrary…", "in fact, the opposite…"
🔍
Illustrates
S2 gives a specific example of the general idea in S1
▾
🔍 ILLUSTRATES — S2 is a specific instance of the general claim in S1

S1 makes a broad, general statement. S2 provides a concrete, specific example that shows what S1 looks like in practice. S2 does not prove S1 — it simply shows one case of it.

Example

S1: The government announced strict measures to control pollution in major cities.

S2: Several industries have already begun adopting cleaner technologies to reduce emissions.

→ S2 shows one specific instance of what is happening in response to S1's general policy — it illustrates it.

Signal Patterns in S2
  • S1 is general → S2 is specific (a named person, place, industry, event)
  • S2 uses "for instance", "for example", "such as", "one such…"
  • S2 could begin: "In Bengaluru…", "The Indian Army…", "One factory…"
⚠️
Qualifies
S2 adds a condition, limitation or exception to S1
▾
⚠️ QUALIFIES — S2 limits or restricts the claim in S1

S2 does not contradict S1 — it accepts S1 as broadly true but adds a condition, exception, or restriction. S2 says: "Yes, but only if…" or "Yes, except when…"

Example

S1: Digital technology has transformed communication across the world.

S2: However, millions in remote rural areas still lack access to stable internet connectivity.

→ S2 accepts S1 but qualifies it with a significant limitation — the transformation is not universal.

Signal Patterns in S2
  • Words: "however", "but", "except", "unless", "only when", "not always"
  • S2 mentions a group, region, or condition where S1 does not apply
  • S2 agrees with S1 in general but carves out an exception
🔭
Generalises
S2 draws a broader principle from the specific idea in S1
▾
🔭 GENERALISES — S2 widens the scope of what S1 says

This is the reverse of Illustrates. S1 is specific; S2 draws a wider, universal conclusion from it. S2 takes the particular case in S1 and extends it to a general rule or pattern.

Example

S1: The soldier continued to march despite a fractured knee, refusing to fall behind his unit.

S2: Courage and duty often compel individuals to push far beyond their physical limits.

→ S2 takes the specific act in S1 and generalises it into a universal principle about courage.

Signal Patterns in S2
  • S1 is a specific case → S2 is a universal/general statement
  • S2 uses "always", "in general", "as a rule", "this shows that…", "such behaviour…"
  • S2 replaces specific nouns with general ones (soldier → individuals)
Section 3 — Three More Relationship Types
❓
Questions
S2 raises a doubt or challenge about the claim in S1
▾
❓ QUESTIONS — S2 casts doubt on whether S1 is achievable or valid

S2 does not directly contradict S1 (i.e., it does not say S1 is factually wrong), but it raises a concern, doubt, or challenge that makes S1 harder to accept without reservation. It asks "but can this really work?" or "but at what cost?"

Example

S1: The administration plans to achieve 100% literacy in the district within two years.

S2: With a shortage of over 3,000 trained teachers and inadequate school infrastructure, the timeline appears highly optimistic.

→ S2 does not say the goal is impossible, but questions its feasibility.

Signal Patterns in S2
  • S2 points to obstacles, resource gaps, or contradicting conditions
  • Words: "however", "yet", "despite", "this raises the question of…"
  • S2 often ends with doubt-language: "seems unlikely", "remains unclear", "is debatable"
🔄
Restates
S2 says the same thing as S1 but in different words
▾
🔄 RESTATES — S2 is a paraphrase or rephrasing of S1

S2 adds no new information. The same idea is expressed using different vocabulary, structure, or emphasis. This is the only type where both sentences are essentially saying exactly the same thing.

Example

S1: Lack of sleep impairs a soldier's judgement and reaction time in the field.

S2: Sleep deprivation reduces a combatant's cognitive sharpness and slows their response to threats.

→ S2 is simply a restatement of S1 using different words (lack of sleep = sleep deprivation; judgement = cognitive sharpness; reaction time = response to threats).

💡
Infers / Concludes
S2 draws a logical conclusion from what S1 states
▾
💡 INFERS — S2 is a deduction or conclusion drawn from S1

S1 provides a premise, observation, or fact. S2 is a logical conclusion drawn from that premise. S2 could not stand on its own without S1 — it follows from S1 by reasoning.

Example

S1: The enemy's supply lines have been cut off and their fuel reserves are critically low.

S2: A retreat or surrender is likely in the coming days.

→ S2 is a logical conclusion drawn from the facts presented in S1.

Signal Patterns in S2
  • Words: "therefore", "thus", "hence", "this means that…", "it can be concluded…"
  • S2 is a prediction, consequence, or deduction — not a new fact
  • S2 would not make sense if you had not read S1 first
Section 4 — Confusable Pairs
⚡
The Four Pairs Most Candidates Mix Up
Side-by-side comparisons with the exact difference
▾
PairHow to Tell Them Apart
Confirms vs Illustrates Confirms = S2 is independent evidence (a study, statistic, parallel event). Illustrates = S2 is a single specific example of S1's general claim. Ask: "Is S2 evidence for S1, or a case of S1?"
Contradicts vs Qualifies Contradicts = S2 says S1 is wrong entirely. Qualifies = S2 says S1 is broadly right but adds an exception or condition. Ask: "Does S2 deny S1 altogether, or just limit its scope?"
Qualifies vs Questions Qualifies = S2 restricts who/when S1 applies to (still partly agrees). Questions = S2 challenges whether S1's aim is realistic or valid. Ask: "Does S2 limit S1, or doubt S1?"
Infers vs Confirms Infers = S2 is a deduction/conclusion from S1 (logical reasoning). Confirms = S2 is a separate factual observation that supports S1. Ask: "Did S2 follow logically from S1, or does S2 exist independently?"
Restates vs Confirms Restates = S2 adds zero new information — same content, different words. Confirms = S2 is a genuinely new piece of supporting evidence. Ask: "Does S2 teach me anything new?"
Generalises vs Infers Generalises = S2 broadens the scope (specific → universal principle). Infers = S2 draws a logical next-step conclusion (cause → predicted effect). Ask: "Is S2 a wider rule, or a specific prediction?"
Sentence Relationships — Complete Quick Reference
All 8 Types at a Glance
  • Confirms — S2 provides supporting evidence for S1
  • Contradicts — S2 directly opposes or disproves S1
  • Illustrates — S2 gives a specific example of S1's general claim
  • Qualifies — S2 limits or adds a condition/exception to S1
  • Generalises — S2 draws a broader principle from S1's specific case
  • Questions — S2 raises doubt about S1's feasibility or validity
  • Restates — S2 rephrases S1 with no new information added
  • Infers — S2 draws a logical conclusion from S1's facts
Confirms — Signal Words in S2
  • Studies / research / data show / confirm / indicate…
  • Reports suggest… / evidence points to…
  • Similarly… / likewise… / in the same way…
  • S2 = independent statistic or parallel fact that agrees with S1
  • Both S1 and S2 can stand as separate, true, supporting facts
Illustrates — Signal Words in S2
  • For example… / for instance… / one such case…
  • S1 is general → S2 names a specific person, place, event
  • S2 could start: "In Delhi…", "The 12th Battalion…", "One factory…"
  • S2 is a case of S1, not proof for S1
Qualifies vs Contradicts
  • Qualifies: "Yes, but only if / except when / not always"
  • S2 words: however, but, except, unless, only in certain cases
  • S2 partially agrees — limits scope, doesn't deny S1 entirely
  • Contradicts: S2 directly opposes — "no, the opposite is true"
  • S2 words: contrary to…, in fact…, the data shows the reverse…
Infers vs Generalises
  • Infers: S2 = logical deduction / prediction / conclusion from S1
  • S2 words: therefore, thus, hence, it follows that, this means…
  • S2 could not make sense without having first read S1
  • Generalises: S2 = wider universal principle drawn from S1's specific case
  • S2 replaces the specific with the general: "soldier → individuals"
The 3-Step Method (Quick)
  • Step 1: Label S1 — is it a fact, claim, policy, problem or observation?
  • Step 2: Label S2 — does it agree, disagree, narrow, widen, or conclude?
  • Step 3: Match to the 8 types using the signal word test
  • Key test: Confirms vs Illustrates → "evidence for" or "case of"?
  • Key test: Qualifies vs Contradicts → "limits" or "denies" S1?
  • Key test: Infers vs Confirms → "deduced from" or "independent of" S1?
