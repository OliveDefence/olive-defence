English Grammar · Pronouns

Pronouns —
Case, Reference & Agreement

Pronouns look simple but are responsible for some of the trickiest errors in CDS, NDA and AFCAT. Wrong case, unclear reference, reflexive misuse — examiners know exactly where candidates slip. This module covers every tested pattern.

Exam Focused CDS · NDA · AFCAT 20 Practice Questions

A pronoun stands in place of a noun. The errors tested in CDS/NDA/AFCAT fall into six clear categories: wrong pronoun case (I vs me), pronoun–antecedent agreement (everyone → their vs his/her), reflexive pronoun misuse (himself used where him is needed), relative pronoun confusion (who vs whom vs whose vs which), ambiguous reference, and miscellaneous traps (it is I, between you and me, etc.). Learn the rule behind each category and the errors become easy to spot.

Section 1 — Pronoun Cases
📋
Subjective vs Objective Case
The foundation — I/he/she/we/they vs me/him/her/us/them
▾

Every personal pronoun has two main forms: the subjective (nominative) form used when the pronoun is the subject of a verb, and the objective (accusative) form used when it is the object of a verb or preposition.

PersonSubjective (Subject)Objective (Object)Possessive

2ndyouyouyour / yours
3rd Singular Mhehimhis
3rd Singular Fsheherher / hers
3rd Singular Nititits

The Isolation Test

When a pronoun appears alongside a noun ("the officers and ___"), remove the noun and test the pronoun alone. The sentence must still sound correct. Example: "The general praised he and the soldiers" → remove "the soldiers" → "The general praised he" — sounds wrong. Correct form: him.

Correct Usage
  • She and I reported for duty. (both are subjects)
  • The CO briefed him and me. (both are objects)
  • Between you and me, the plan was flawed. (objects of preposition)
Classic Errors
  • Her and the team won the award. → She and the team… ✓
  • The CO spoke to I and the cadet. → …to me and the cadet ✓
  • Between you and I, this is wrong. → Between you and me ✓
⚖️
Pronoun after "than" and "as"
Incomplete comparisons — the most misunderstood rule
▾

After "than" or "as" in a comparison, the pronoun case depends on the implied verb that has been omitted.

The Implied Verb Rule

Mentally complete the sentence to identify the role of the pronoun.
"He is taller than I [am]." → "I" is the subject of the implied "am" → subjective case ✓
"The CO trusts her more than [he trusts] me." → "me" is the object of "trusts" → objective case ✓

Meaning Changes with Case
  • "She likes the colonel more than I [do]." → I like him less than she does.
  • "She likes the colonel more than me." → She likes the colonel more than she likes me.
⚑ Exam Trap

In CDS/NDA papers, "He runs faster than me" is often presented as correct. In formal written English (which exams test), the correct form is "than I" because the implied verb is "run" — making the pronoun a subject. Always apply the implied-verb test in Spotting Errors questions.

Section 2 — Pronoun–Antecedent Agreement
🔗
Singular Indefinite Pronouns
Everyone, someone, each, neither, either → always singular
▾

These indefinite pronouns are grammatically singular and must take a singular pronoun to refer back to them. This is one of the most tested rules in CDS/NDA Spotting Errors.

Always Singular Indefinite Pronouns
  • everyone, everybody, everything, someone, somebody, something
  • anyone, anybody, anything, no one, nobody, nothing
  • each, either, neither, one
Correct Usage
  • Everyone must carry his or her identity card. ✓
  • Each soldier was responsible for his own equipment. ✓
  • Neither of the officers submitted his report. ✓
Common Errors
  • Everyone must carry their identity card. ✗ → his or her ✓
  • Each of the cadets cleaned their rifle. ✗ → his rifle ✓
  • Neither officer submitted their report. ✗ → his report ✓
👥
Collective Nouns & Special Antecedents
Army, committee, class, jury — singular or plural?
▾
Antecedent TypePronounExample
Collective noun acting as a unitSingular (it)The jury gave its verdict.
Collective noun acting individuallyPlural (their)The jury were divided in their opinions.
Two nouns joined by "and"Plural (they/their)Ram and Shyam lost their way.
Two nouns joined by "or/nor"Agrees with nearer nounEither the captain or the men lost their way.
"Each / every" before compound subjectSingular (his/her)Every soldier and officer did his duty.
⚑ The "or / nor" Trap

With either…or / neither…nor, the pronoun agrees with the nearer antecedent (the noun closest to the pronoun).
"Neither the colonel nor the soldiers had cleaned their weapons." (soldiers = nearer, plural → their ✓)
"Neither the soldiers nor the colonel had cleaned his weapon." (colonel = nearer, singular → his ✓)

Section 3 — Reflexive Pronouns
🪞
Myself, Yourself, Himself… — Correct Use
Reflexive pronouns have exactly two legitimate uses
▾

Reflexive pronouns (myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, themselves) have only two correct uses. Using them outside these two contexts is an error — and examiners love testing this.

Two Correct Uses

Use 1 — Reflexive: The subject and object are the same person.
"He injured himself during training." (he = himself)

Use 2 — Emphatic: To stress that the subject did something personally.
"The General himself inspected the troops." (emphasis: nobody else, he did it)

Incorrect Uses — Most Tested in CDS/NDA
  • Please contact myself for queries. → contact me ✓
  • The officer spoke to myself and the cadet. → to me ✓
  • Myself and the team will handle it. → I and the team ✓
  • He gave the award to himself and his partner. → to him and his partner ✓ (no reflexive needed; subject ≠ object here)
⚑ The Politeness Trap

Many people write "Please contact myself" thinking it sounds more formal or polite. This is always wrong in standard English. "Myself" cannot replace "me" or "I" simply for the sake of politeness. If you can replace it with "me" or "I" and the sentence still makes sense, use "me" or "I" — not "myself".

Section 4 — Relative Pronouns
🔍
Who, Whom, Whose, Which, That
The most error-prone group — especially who vs whom
▾
PronounUsed forCaseExample
whoPeopleSubjectThe officer who led the mission was decorated.
whomPeopleObjectThe officer whom they praised was decorated.
whosePeople & thingsPossessiveThe soldier whose rifle jammed was replaced.
whichAnimals & thingsSub / ObjThe tank, which had broken down, was repaired.
thatPeople, animals, thingsSub / ObjThe plan that was approved was executed well.
Who vs Whom — The He/Him Test

Substitute "he" or "him" in the relative clause.
If "he" fits → use who.   If "him" fits → use whom.

"The officer ___ won the medal" → he won the medal → who ✓
"The officer ___ they selected" → they selected him → whom ✓

⚑ Which vs That — Defining vs Non-Defining
  • "That" is used in defining (restrictive) clauses — no commas: The gun that misfired was replaced.
  • "Which" is used in non-defining (non-restrictive) clauses — with commas: The gun, which had misfired once before, was replaced.
  • Never use "which" without commas in a defining clause in CDS/NDA formal English.
Section 5 — High-Frequency Exam Traps
⚡
It Is I / It Is Me — and Other Predicative Traps
After linking verbs, formal English demands the subjective case
▾

After linking verbs (is, was, were, am, are, be), the pronoun that follows is technically a predicate nominative and must be in the subjective case.

Correct Formal Forms
  • It is I who made the error. (not "me")
  • It was he who gave the order. (not "him")
  • It was they who arrived first. (not "them")
More High-Frequency Traps
  • "Between you and me" is always correct — never "between you and I" (both are objects of the preposition "between")
  • "Let him and me go" — not "him and I" (objects of "let")
  • "It is I" in formal/exam English; "It's me" only in informal speech
  • After "but" used as a preposition meaning "except": "No one but me was present." (object of "but")
  • After "like" and "unlike": "Like him, she excelled." (object of preposition)
🎯
Ambiguous Pronoun Reference
When "he", "it" or "they" could refer to more than one noun
▾

A pronoun must refer clearly to one and only one antecedent. If the reader cannot tell with certainty which noun the pronoun refers to, the sentence has an ambiguous pronoun reference error.

Ambiguous Reference Errors
  • "When the colonel met the general, he saluted him."
    → Who saluted whom? "he" and "him" are both unclear. Rewrite: "The colonel saluted the general when they met."
  • "Ram told Shyam that he had failed the test."
    → "he" could be Ram or Shyam. Rewrite: "Ram told Shyam, 'I failed the test.'"
Also Tested: Broad / Vague Reference
  • Using "this", "that", "it", "which" to refer to a whole clause or idea — not to a single noun — creates a vague reference error
  • He was late, which annoyed the CO. → His lateness annoyed the CO. ✓ ("which" must refer to a specific noun, not the whole clause)
  • In exam Spotting Errors, look for a pronoun whose antecedent is a clause, not a noun
Pronouns — Complete Quick Reference
Case at a Glance
  • Subject position: I, he, she, we, they
  • Object position: me, him, her, us, them
  • After prepositions → always objective: between you and me
  • After linking verbs → subjective: It is I
  • Isolation test: remove paired noun and read pronoun alone
  • "than / as" comparisons → apply implied-verb test
Singular Indefinite Pronouns (→ singular verb & pronoun)
  • everyone, everybody, everything
  • someone, somebody, something
  • anyone, anybody, anything
  • no one, nobody, nothing
  • each, either, neither, one
  • All take: his / her / his or her — never "their"
Reflexive Pronouns — Only 2 Correct Uses
  • Reflexive: subject & object are same — He hurt himself
  • Emphatic: to stress subject did it — The CO himself checked
  • Never as a substitute for I / me: Contact myself ✗
  • Never as subject: Myself and the team… ✗ → I and the team ✓
Who vs Whom (He/Him Test)
  • Substitute he → use who | substitute him → use whom
  • The officer who led… (he led ✓)
  • The officer whom they chose… (they chose him ✓)
  • Whose = possessive (people & things)
  • Which = non-defining clause (commas required)
  • That = defining clause (no commas)
Or / Nor Agreement Rule
  • Pronoun agrees with the nearer antecedent
  • Neither the men nor the colonel did his duty. (colonel = nearer, singular)
  • Neither the colonel nor the men did their duty. (men = nearer, plural)
  • Each / Every before compound subject → singular pronoun
  • Every soldier and officer did his duty.
High-Frequency Fixed Rules
  • Between you and me — never "I" ✗
  • It is I — formal; "It's me" only informal
  • Let him and me go — not "he and I" ✗
  • No one but me — "but" = preposition here
  • Like him, she excelled. — "like" = preposition
  • "which" cannot refer to a whole clause → rewrite with a noun
