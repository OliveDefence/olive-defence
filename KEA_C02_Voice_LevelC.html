Voice — Active & Passive
Who does the action and who receives it — mastering the verb "To Be," all passive transformations, and the rules of voice across tenses. Essential for Level C grammar mastery.
Level C Active Voice Passive Voice Verb To Be V3 Forms

What is Voice? In English grammar, Voice tells us whether the subject of a sentence is performing the action or receiving it. There are two voices: Active (subject does the action) and Passive (subject receives the action).

To change voice, we need the verb "To Be" — the most important helping verb in English. Understanding its forms across tenses is the key to mastering passive voice.

Part 1 — Active & Passive Voice
🟢
Active Voice
Subject → does the action · Direct & clear · Most common in everyday writing
▾

Use Active Voice when you want to show who is performing the action clearly. It is direct, energetic, and easier to read.

Structure
Subject + Verb + Object
The subject performs the action directly.
Examples — Active Voice
  • Arjun kicks the football. — Arjun (S) → kicks (V) → football (O)
  • The farmer in Kashmir grows saffron every year.
  • Meera wrote a beautiful poem about the Dal Lake.
  • The chef prepared delicious kahwa for the guests.
🔵
Passive Voice
Subject ← receives the action · Uses "to be" + V3 · Formal & scientific writing
▾

Use Passive Voice when the action is more important than who does it, when the doer is unknown, or in formal/scientific writing.

Structure
Subject + To Be + V3 (Past Participle) + (by + agent)
The doer is placed after "by" — and is optional.
Examples — Passive Voice
  • The football is kicked by Arjun. — football receives the action
  • Saffron is grown in Kashmir every year. — doer not mentioned
  • A beautiful poem about the Dal Lake was written by Meera.
  • The road to Gulmarg was blocked by heavy snow.
Active vs Passive — The Spotlight

Active voice shines the spotlight on the doer. Passive voice moves the spotlight to the action or the object. Newspapers often use passive when the doer is unknown: "The bridge was damaged." (We don't know who — or it doesn't matter.)

Part 2 — Active → Passive Transformation (Tense-wise)
🔄
Tense-wise Transformations
Simple Present · Simple Past · Future · Continuous · Perfect
▾
Simple Present → is/am/are + V3
Rohan eats mangoes.
↓ passive
Mangoes are eaten by Rohan.
The teacher explains the lesson.
↓
The lesson is explained by the teacher.
Simple Past → was/were + V3
Zara painted the wall.
↓ passive
The wall was painted by Zara.
The floods damaged the bridge near Anantnag.
↓
The bridge near Anantnag was damaged by the floods.
Simple Future → will be + V3
Priya will bake the cake.
↓ passive
The cake will be baked by Priya.
The government will build a new school in Baramulla.
↓
A new school will be built in Baramulla by the government.
Present Continuous → is/am/are being + V3
The workers are building the bridge.
↓ passive
The bridge is being built by the workers.
Present Perfect → has/have been + V3
Aisha has written the letter.
↓ passive
The letter has been written by Aisha.
Past Perfect → had been + V3
The team had finished the project.
↓ passive
The project had been finished by the team.
📋
5 Rules for Changing Active to Passive
Step-by-step method — never get it wrong
▾
The 5 Steps
  • Step 1 — The Object of active becomes the Subject of passive. Active: Priya (S) wrote the letter (O). → Passive: The letter (S)…
  • Step 2 — The verb changes to: correct form of "to be" + Past Participle (V3). The tense decides which form of "to be."
  • Step 3 — The active Subject becomes "by + agent" phrase at the end (optional — omit if unknown).
  • Step 4 — Change pronouns: I→me, he→him, she→her, they→them, we→us (object form after "by"). Active: She helped me. → Passive: I was helped by her.
  • Step 5 — ⚠️ Intransitive verbs (no object) CANNOT be made passive. "The bird flew" — no passive possible.
⚑ Common Mistakes
  • ❌ "by she / by he / by they" → ✅ "by her / by him / by them" — use object pronouns after "by"
  • ❌ "The cake is baked yesterday." → ✅ "The cake was baked yesterday." — match "to be" to original tense
  • ❌ "The letter was write by her." → ✅ "The letter was written by her." — always use V3, not V1/V2
Part 3 — The Verb "To Be" & Its Forms
🟣
All 8 Forms of "To Be"
am · is · are · was · were · be · been · being
▾

"To Be" works in three ways: (1) as a Main Verb — describing a state; (2) as an Auxiliary — forming continuous tenses; (3) as a Passive Auxiliary — forming all passive sentences.

Three Roles — Examples
  • Arjun is a good student. → Main verb (state of being)
  • She is singing a folk song. → Auxiliary (forms present continuous)
  • The book was written by Rahul. → Passive auxiliary (was + V3)
  • Meena has been waiting for an hour. → Perfect continuous (been + V-ing)
Passive Formation Using "To Be"

Present Simple Passive: is/am/are + V3 → "The letter is written."
Past Simple Passive: was/were + V3 → "The letter was written."
Future Passive: will be + V3 → "The letter will be written."
Continuous Passive: is/am/are being + V3 → "The road is being repaired."
Perfect Passive: has/have/had been + V3 → "The letter has been sent."

🟢 Active Voice
  • Subject does the action
  • Structure: S + V + O
  • Direct and clear — most common in speech
  • Emphasises the doer
🔵 Passive Voice
  • Subject receives the action
  • "To be" + V3 — always
  • "by + agent" is optional
  • Used in formal, news, and scientific writing
  • Doer unknown or unimportant → omit "by…"
🟣 "To Be" — Present Forms
  • I → am
  • He / She / It → is
  • You / We / They → are
🟣 "To Be" — Past & Other Forms
  • I / He / She / It → was
  • You / We / They → were
  • After modal (will/can/must) → be
  • After have / has / had → been
  • Continuous passive → being

⚑ Critical Rules — Never Forget
  • Passive always needs "to be" + V3 — never V1 or V2
  • ❌ "by she/he/they" → ✅ "by her/him/them"
  • Intransitive verbs (sleep, die, go, cry…) → NO passive
  • Match "to be" to the original tense
  • After "have/has/had" always use "been" (not "be" or "being")
  • "being" = continuous/progressive passive only
