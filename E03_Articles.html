English Grammar · Articles

Articles —
A, An & The

Articles seem simple, but they are among the most consistently tested topics in CDS, NDA & AFCAT — in Spotting Errors, Fill in the Blanks and Sentence Improvement, every single year.

Beginner Friendly CDS · NDA · AFCAT 20 Practice Questions

An article is a type of determiner placed before a noun. English has three articles: A, An (indefinite) and The (definite). The most common errors tested in CDS/NDA/AFCAT are: using "a" vs "an" incorrectly, omitting or inserting "the" wrongly, and confusion with abstract/uncountable nouns. This module covers every pattern you need.

Section 1 — What Are Articles?
📖
The Basic Idea
Definite vs Indefinite — at a glance
▾

Articles tell us whether a noun is specific or general. They are the most frequently used words in English, yet they are misused constantly in exam sentences.

ArticleTypeUseExample
AIndefiniteBefore consonant sounds — one, anyI saw a dog.
AnIndefiniteBefore vowel sounds — one, anyShe is an engineer.
TheDefiniteSpecific or previously mentioned nounThe dog I saw was black.
The Golden Rule

A/An depends on the sound of the next word — NOT the letter. If the word begins with a vowel sound, use an. If it begins with a consonant sound, use a. This distinction catches hundreds of exam candidates every year.

Section 2 — A vs An: Sound, Not Spelling
🔊
The Sound Rule
It's always about pronunciation, never just the letter
▾

The choice between "a" and "an" depends entirely on the initial sound of the word that follows — not its first letter.

Vowel Sound → Use "An"
  • an officer (o- = vowel sound ✓)
  • an honest man (h is silent → starts with 'o' sound ✓)
  • an hour (h is silent → starts with 'au' sound ✓)
  • an MBA degree (M → sounds like "em" = vowel sound ✓)
  • an unusual event (u sounds like "un" = vowel sound ✓)
Consonant Sound → Use "A"
  • a uniform (u sounds like "yoo" = consonant sound ✓)
  • a university (u sounds like "yoo" ✓)
  • a one-rupee coin (o sounds like "wun" = consonant sound ✓)
  • a European country (E sounds like "yoo" = consonant sound ✓)
  • a historical event (h is pronounced ✓)
⚑ Exam Trap — Abbreviations & Acronyms
  • Use the sound of the first letter when spoken aloud: "an NDA officer" (N = "en", vowel sound)
  • an FIR, an MLA, an SSB interview — all start with vowel-sounding letters
  • a BSc degree, a CDS candidate — B and C start with consonant sounds ("bee", "see"... wait — "see" is a vowel sound! So: an SSC exam)
Section 3 — Indefinite Article "A/An": Key Uses
🔢
When to Use A / An
Six exam-tested patterns
▾
RuleExample
First mention of a singular countable nounI bought a book.
Referring to one of a class/professionHe is an engineer.
Meaning "per" (rate expressions)She earns ₹500 a day.
With "such a", "quite a", "rather a"It was such a difficult paper.
With a proper noun used as common nounHe is a Newton of our times.
After exclamations — "What a…!"What a brave soldier!
Do NOT use A/An with
  • Uncountable/mass nouns: ✗ a water, a rice, a music
  • Plural nouns: ✗ a soldiers, an officers
  • Abstract nouns used in general sense: ✗ a honesty, a courage
Section 4 — Definite Article "The": Core Rules
📌
When to Use "The"
Most tested rules in CDS/NDA spotting errors
▾

Use "the" when the noun is specific, unique, or already known to the listener/reader.

RuleExample
Second mention (already introduced)I saw a dog. The dog barked.
Unique/one-of-a-kind thingsthe sun, the moon, the earth
Superlative degreeHe is the best cadet in the batch.
Ordinal numbersthe first, the second, the last
Names of rivers, seas, oceans, mountain rangesthe Ganges, the Himalayas, the Pacific
Names of countries that are unions/groupsthe USA, the UAE, the Netherlands
Musical instrumentsShe plays the violin.
Whole class represented by singular nounThe tiger is a powerful animal.
Newspapers, religious books, shipsThe Hindustan Times, The Quran
🚫
When NOT to Use "The"
Omission errors — the other half of the trap
▾

Just as important as knowing when to use "the" is knowing when to leave it out. These omission rules are heavily tested.

Do NOT use "The" before…Example
Names of languagesShe speaks Hindi. (not "the Hindi")
Names of games/sportsHe plays cricket. (not "the cricket")
Names of single mountains or lakesMount Everest, Lake Dal (not "the Everest")
Names of mealsWe had dinner at 8.
Abstract nouns used in general senseHonesty is the best policy.
Proper nouns (names of people)Captain Sharma arrived.
Names of subjects/disciplinesShe studied Physics.
⚑ The Biggest Trap — "The" with Proper Nouns
  • No "the" before a person's name: The Arjun won the medal ✗ → Arjun won the medal ✓
  • But: "the Arjun of our team" (using a name as a common comparison) is correct
  • No "the" before names of single cities, countries (except unions): The India is great ✗
Section 5 — Special & Tricky Cases
🎯
Tricky Article Patterns
High-frequency traps from past papers
▾
The "Double Superlative" Trap
  • "most" without "the" when used as adverb: She was most helpful (= very helpful — no "the")
  • "the most" when comparing: She was the most helpful person in the room ✓
School / College / Hospital — With or Without "The"?
  • No "the" → purpose/function: He went to school (to study) | She is in hospital (as a patient)
  • Use "the" → specific place: He went to the school to meet the principal | The school is near our house
Article with Adjective + Noun
  • When an adjective comes before the noun, the article goes before the adjective: a brave soldier, an old officer
  • Exception — "quite/rather/such/what + a/an + adj + noun": quite a difficult task, what an excellent idea
  • Note: "half a/an" → half an hour; but "a half" is also correct in informal use
Most-Tested Error Types
  • He is best player in the team. → missing "the": He is the best player… ✓
  • She plays the cricket. → no article with sports: She plays cricket. ✓
  • He gave a useful advise. → "advice" is uncountable: He gave useful advice. ✓
  • The honesty is the best policy. → abstract noun in general sense: Honesty is the best policy. ✓
  • He is an university student. → "u" in university = "yoo" sound: He is a university student. ✓

Articles — Complete Quick Reference
A vs An — Sound Rule
  • an honest man — h is silent (vowel sound)
  • an hour — h is silent (vowel sound)
  • a uniform — 'u' = 'yoo' (consonant sound)
  • a university — 'u' = 'yoo' (consonant sound)
  • a one-way street — 'o' = 'wun' (consonant sound)
  • a European tour — 'E' = 'yoo' (consonant sound)
  • an NDA officer — N = 'en' (vowel sound)
  • an MBA — M = 'em' (vowel sound)
Always Use "The"
  • Superlatives: the best, the tallest, the most
  • Ordinals: the first, the second, the last
  • Unique things: the sun, the moon, the earth
  • Rivers/Ranges/Oceans: the Ganges, the Himalayas
  • Musical instruments: play the guitar
  • Specific/previously mentioned nouns
  • Country unions: the USA, the UK, the UAE
Never Use "The"
  • Languages: Hindi, English, French
  • Sports & games: cricket, chess, football
  • Meals: breakfast, lunch, dinner
  • Academic subjects: Physics, History, Maths
  • Single mountains/lakes: Mount Everest, Lake Dal
  • Abstract nouns in general: Honesty, Courage, Love
  • Most countries: India, France, Japan
  • People's names: Captain Singh, Priya
School / Hospital / Church — Special Rule
  • No article — when visiting for its primary purpose
  • go to school (to study), in hospital (as patient)
  • "The" — when visiting as an outsider/for another purpose
  • go to the school to meet principal
  • went to the hospital to see a friend
Commonly Confused — Exam Traps
  • a/an + singular countable noun (first mention)
  • He is a unique talent — unique starts with 'yoo'
  • It was an ugly scene — ugly starts with vowel
  • What + a/an: What a wonderful day!
  • Such + a/an: Such an odd question!
  • half an hour — h is silent
  • once in a while — fixed expression
Key Formula Patterns
  • the + comparative + S + V, the + comparative + S + V
  • The more you practise, the better you get.
  • by + article + noun: paid by the hour
  • in + article + phrase: in the morning / in the evening
  • BUT: at night (no article) — fixed expression
  • a number of (plural verb) vs the number of (singular verb)
