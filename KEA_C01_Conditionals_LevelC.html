The Conditionals
Zero, First, Second & Third — understanding conditions and results, imagined worlds and real possibilities. Essential for Level C grammar mastery.
Level C Zero First Second Third If Clauses

What is a Conditional Sentence? A conditional sentence tells us about a condition (something that must happen) and its result. These sentences often use the word "if" to connect two parts.

Think of it like this: "If Arjun studies, he will pass." The studying is the condition; passing is the result. In English grammar, we have four main types of conditionals — each describes a different kind of situation.

The Four Types of Conditionals
🟢
Zero Conditional — General Truths & Scientific Facts
Always true · Both clauses in Simple Present
▾

Use the Zero Conditional for facts that are always true — scientific laws, general habits, or things that happen every time a certain condition is met. There is no "maybe" here.

Formula
If + Simple Present , Simple Present
OR: Simple Present  if  Simple Present
Examples — General Truths
  • If you heat water to 100°C, it boils. — scientific truth
  • If Aarav eats too many walnuts from Shopian, he gets a stomachache. — personal habit
  • Plants die if they do not get sunlight. — "if" clause can come second
  • If it snows heavily in Gulmarg, the roads close. — regular occurrence
Memory Tip — "Always!"

Both verbs = Simple Present. Think: facts you'd find in a science textbook. If + V1 … V1. "When" can replace "if" in Zero Conditionals.

🔵
First Conditional — Real & Possible Future
Likely to happen · Simple Present in "if" clause · Will + V1 in result
▾

Use the First Conditional for situations that are possible and likely to happen in the future. The speaker believes the condition could really happen.

Formula
If + Simple Present , will + Base Verb
Result can also use: can / may / might / should instead of will
Examples — Real Possibilities
  • If Meera studies hard, she will top the class 6 exam. — possible!
  • If the orchards in Kashmir get enough rain, farmers will earn good profits.
  • Rahul will miss the bus if he does not wake up early. — "if" clause can come second
  • If Zara visits the Mughal Gardens, she will take many photos.
⚑ Critical Rule
  • NEVER use "will" inside the "if" clause: ❌ "If she will study…" → ✅ "If she studies…"
  • The "if" clause always uses Simple Present, even though it refers to the future.
🟡
Second Conditional — Unreal or Unlikely Present/Future
Imaginary · Simple Past in "if" clause · Would + V1 in result
▾

Use the Second Conditional for imaginary, unlikely, or impossible present/future situations — dreams, wishes, and hypothetical ideas. The condition is NOT currently true.

Formula
If + Simple Past , would + Base Verb
Use "were" (not "was") for all persons in formal/exam writing
Examples — Imaginary Situations
  • If I were the principal, I would give extra holidays during Eid. — I am not the principal
  • If Rohan had a magic carpet, he would fly from Jammu to Leh in seconds. — impossible
  • If Priya lived near the Dal Lake, she would go shikara riding every morning.
  • What would you do if you found a snow leopard near your school in Pahalgam?
Were vs Was — Formal Rule

In formal writing and exams, always use "were" for all subjects in Second Conditional: "If I were rich… / If she were here… / If he were taller…". This is called the subjunctive mood.

🔴
Third Conditional — Unreal Past (Regrets & Imagined Past)
Impossible now · Past Perfect in "if" clause · Would have + V3 in result
▾

Use the Third Conditional to talk about past situations that did NOT happen. We imagine a different past — often expressing regret or relief about what actually occurred.

Formula
If + Past Perfect (had + V₃) , would have + V₃
Can also use: could have / might have + V3
Examples — Imagined Past / Regrets
  • If Arjun had practised cricket every day, he would have been selected for the school team. — he did NOT practise
  • If the tourists had visited Sonamarg earlier, they would have seen the full bloom of flowers.
  • Aisha would have won the spelling competition if she had revised her words carefully.
  • If the chef had added more Kashmiri saffron, the kahwa would have tasted even better.
The "If Only…" Feeling

Third Conditional always carries the feeling of "If only things had been different." The past cannot be changed now — we are just imagining it. If you feel regret when you read the sentence, it's Third Conditional.

⚑ Double Past — Easy to Confuse
  • ❌ "If he had studied, he would pass." → ✅ "…he would have passed." (must have + V3)
  • ❌ "If she studied harder, she would have passed." → Mix of 2nd & 3rd is wrong. Keep consistent.
  • Remember: Third Conditional = TWO past forms: had + V3 and would have + V3

🟢 Zero Conditional — "Always True"
  • Situation: Scientific facts, habits, universal truths
  • If clause: Simple Present
  • Result clause: Simple Present
  • Formula: If + V1 … V1
  • "when" can replace "if" in Zero Conditionals
  • Key words: always, every time, generally, usually
🔵 First Conditional — "It Can Happen!"
  • Situation: Real and likely future possibilities
  • If clause: Simple Present (NEVER will)
  • Result clause: will / can / may + V1
  • Formula: If + V1 … will + V1
  • Key words: tomorrow, soon, probably, definitely
  • ❌ "If she will come…" → ✅ "If she comes…"
🟡 Second Conditional — "Just Imagining…"
  • Situation: Imaginary, unlikely, or impossible present/future
  • If clause: Simple Past
  • Result clause: would / could / might + V1
  • Formula: If + V2 … would + V1
  • Use "were" (not "was") for all subjects formally
  • Key words: if only, wish, would, imagine
🔴 Third Conditional — "If Only…" (Regret)
  • Situation: Unreal past — it didn't happen
  • If clause: Past Perfect (had + V3)
  • Result clause: would have / could have + V3
  • Formula: If + had V3 … would have V3
  • Key words: would have, could have, had + V3
  • Feeling: regret or relief about the past
⚑ Critical Rules — Never Forget
  • NEVER use "will" in the "if" clause of First Conditional
  • Second Conditional uses Simple Past — NOT past perfect
  • Second Conditional: use "were" not "was" in formal English
  • Third Conditional needs BOTH: had + V3 AND would have + V3
  • The "if" clause can come first OR second in any conditional
  • When "if" clause is second, no comma is needed
