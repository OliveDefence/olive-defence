What is a verb? Action, state & helping verbs — understanding the verb before you learn tenses. Affirmative, negative, and question sentences. An essential foundation for Class 3–4.
Class 3–4 Action Verbs Helping Verbs V1 V2 V3 V4 V5 Questions

Before we learn tenses, we need to understand the verb — the most important word in any English sentence. A sentence can exist without a noun, but never without a verb. The verb is the engine. Everything else in the sentence depends on it.

In this lesson, we will learn: what a verb is, the three types of verbs, the five forms every verb has, and how to build positive, negative, and question sentences using helping verbs.

Part 1 — What Is a Verb?
⚡
The Verb — Heart of Every Sentence
Every complete sentence must have a verb. No exceptions.
▾

A verb is a word that tells us what the subject does, is, or has. It gives life and meaning to a sentence. Without a verb, a group of words is just a phrase — not a sentence.

🚂 The Train Analogy

Think of a sentence as a train. The subject is the engine driver. The verb is the engine itself — it makes the train move. The object is the cargo. Without the engine (verb), the train goes nowhere.

Sentences — Spot the Verb
  • Arjun runs every morning near the Dal Lake. — verb = runs
  • The saffron grows in the fields of Pampore. — verb = grows
  • Meera is a clever student. — verb = is (state)
  • They are playing cricket in the garden. — verb = are playing (two verbs working together)
Quick Test — Is It a Verb?
  • Can you say "I ___ it" with the word? If yes — it's a verb!
  • I eat it ✅ · I sleep ✅ · I happy ❌ (happy is an adjective, not a verb)
  • Some verbs do not need an object: "The baby sleeps." is a complete sentence.

Action Verbs — "Doing Words"

Action verbs tell us about physical or mental activities. You can "perform" them. They are the most common type of verb. Examples: run, eat, write, think, decide, build, sing, help.

State Verbs — "Being and Having Words"

State verbs describe a condition or state — not an activity. They include: is/am/are/was/were (to be), have/has/had (to have), and feeling/thinking words like know, believe, love, seem, belong, own. These verbs are not usually used in the continuous (-ing) form.

★ Helping Verbs — "The Assistants"
  • Helping verbs (also called auxiliary verbs) work together with a main verb to build a complete tense. They cannot usually stand alone as the only verb.
  • Example: "She is singing." — "is" helps "singing" form the present continuous tense.
  • Example: "They did not come." — "did" helps make the sentence negative in simple past.
  • You will use helping verbs in every tense you will ever learn. They are the most important function words in English grammar.
Part 2 — The Five Forms of a Verb
📐
V1 V2 V3 V4 V5 — Every Verb Has Five Forms
Base · Simple Past · Past Participle · Present Participle · Third Person Singular
▾

Every English verb has five forms. When you learn a new verb, you should know all five forms — especially V1, V2, and V3, which are used in almost every tense.

Form Name When Used play write go
V1Base FormPresent, future, after do/does/did/will/canplaywritego
V2Simple PastPast tense (yesterday, last week, ago)playedwrotewent
V3Past ParticipleAfter have/has/had · Passive voiceplayedwrittengone
V4Present ParticipleAfter is/am/are/was/were (continuous)playingwritinggoing
V5Third Person SingularWith he/she/it in Simple Presentplayswritesgoes
🔮 Why Do Forms Matter?

Different tenses need different forms. Think of V1/V2/V3/V4/V5 as different outfits for the same verb. You pick the outfit that matches the tense you're using. When you learn a tense, you'll always be told which form (V1, V2, or V3) to wear.

Regular vs Irregular
  • Regular verbs: V2 and V3 are both formed by adding -ed. play → played → played. Easy!
  • Irregular verbs: V2 and V3 do NOT follow the -ed rule. go → went → gone. These must be memorised. See the Verb Forms tab for the full list.
  • V4 is almost always V1 + -ing (with minor spelling changes): write → writing · run → running.
  • V5 is almost always V1 + -s or -es: play → plays · go → goes · watch → watches.

What Helping Verbs Do

is/am/are/was/were — form continuous tenses ("is running") and passive voice.
do/does/did — form negatives ("does not eat") and questions ("Does she eat?") in simple tenses.
have/has/had — form perfect tenses ("has finished").
will — forms the future tense ("will come").

Part 5 — Negative Sentences (Adding "Not")
❌
Negative Sentences — Using "Not"
do not / does not / did not / is not / are not / was not / will not
▾

To make a sentence negative, we add "not" after the helping verb. If there is no helping verb, we must bring in "do / does / did" to carry the "not".

The "Not" Rule — Simple Present Negative

Subject + do not (don't) / does not (doesn't) + V1 (base form) + Object
⚠️ When you add "do/does", the main verb goes back to V1 — never V5!
❌ "She does not plays." → ✅ "She does not play."

⚑ Most Common Beginner Mistakes
  • ❌ "She does not plays." → ✅ "She does not play." (V1 after does not)
  • ❌ "He did not went." → ✅ "He did not go." (V1 after did not)
  • ❌ "They not like it." → ✅ "They do not like it." (need "do/does")
  • ❌ "I not am hungry." → ✅ "I am not hungry." ("not" comes AFTER "to be")
Part 6 — Interrogative Sentences (Questions & Inversion)
❓
Questions — Inverting Subject and Verb
Inversion: Helping Verb moves before the Subject
▾

In English, to ask a question, we invert (swap) the helping verb and the subject. The helping verb jumps to the front, before the subject. This is called inversion.

🔄 Inversion — The Swap

Statement: She is singing. → Question: Is she singing?
The helping verb (is) swaps place with the subject (she). That swap is called inversion. It happens in every question in English.

Wh- Questions — The Same Inversion + a Question Word

Wh- words (what, where, when, who, why, how) come at the very start, THEN the inverted structure follows.

Wh-word + Helping Verb + Subject + V1 + Object ?

Where does she live?  ·  What did you eat?  ·  When will they arrive?

⚑ Inversion Mistakes to Avoid
  • ❌ "Where she lives?" → ✅ "Where does she live?" — inversion is compulsory!
  • ❌ "Did she went?" → ✅ "Did she go?" — V1 after "did", never V2
  • ❌ "Does he plays?" → ✅ "Does he play?" — V1 after "does"
Part 7 — Connecting Verbs to Tenses
🔮
How Verb Forms Map to Tenses
This is the foundation for everything you will learn in grammar
▾

Every tense you will ever learn uses a specific verb form + helping verb combination. Once you know the five verb forms and the helping verbs, learning any new tense becomes a simple matter of combining them correctly.

Helping Verb + Verb Form = Tense
TenseFormulaExampleVerb Form
Simple PresentV1 (or V5)She plays.V1 / V5
Present Continuousis/am/are + V4She is playing.V4 (-ing)
Simple PastV2 aloneShe played.V2
Past Continuouswas/were + V4She was playing.V4 (-ing)
Present Perfecthave/has + V3She has played.V3
Past Perfecthad + V3She had played.V3
Simple Futurewill + V1She will play.V1
★ The Big Picture
  • V1 is the most versatile — used in Present, Future, and after all do/does/did/will/can.
  • V2 is the simple past — used alone, no helping verb needed in affirmatives.
  • V3 (past participle) is used after have/has/had for perfect tenses, and in passive voice.
  • V4 (-ing form) is used after is/am/are/was/were for continuous (ongoing) tenses.
  • Negative and questions in simple tenses always need do/does/did + V1.

⚡ Three Types of Verbs
  • Action verb: tells what someone does — run, eat, write, sing
  • State verb: tells a condition — is, am, are, have, know, love
  • Helping verb: assists the main verb — is, do, does, did, will, have
  • State verbs are NOT normally used in continuous (-ing) form
📐 The Five Verb Forms
  • V1 — Base form: play, write, go
  • V2 — Simple Past: played, wrote, went
  • V3 — Past Participle: played, written, gone
  • V4 — Present Participle (-ing): playing, writing, going
  • V5 — Third Person Singular (-s/-es): plays, writes, goes
  • Regular: V2 = V3 = V1 + -ed   |   Irregular: must memorise
✅ Affirmative Sentences
  • Simple Present: S + V1/V5 + O → She plays.
  • Pres. Continuous: S + is/am/are + V4 → She is playing.
  • Simple Past: S + V2 + O → She played.
  • Simple Future: S + will + V1 + O → She will play.
❌ Negative Sentences
  • Simple Present (I/you/we/they): S + do not + V1 + O
  • Simple Present (he/she/it): S + does not + V1 + O
  • Pres. Continuous: S + is/am/are + not + V4
  • Simple Past: S + did not + V1 + O
  • Simple Future: S + will not + V1 + O
  • ⚠️ After do/does/did → always V1, never V2 or V5!
❓ Interrogative (Questions) — Inversion
  • Simple Present: Do/Does + S + V1 + O + ?
  • Pres. Continuous: Is/Am/Are + S + V4 + ?
  • Simple Past: Did + S + V1 + O + ?
  • Simple Future: Will + S + V1 + O + ?
  • Wh- questions: Wh-word + Helping Verb + S + V1 + ?
  • Inversion = helping verb moves before the subject.
🤝 Helping Verb Quick Guide
  • am / is / are / was / were → continuous tenses + passive
  • do / does → simple present negatives & questions
  • did → simple past negatives & questions
  • have / has / had → perfect tenses
  • will → future tense (all subjects)
  • does/did → main verb must be V1 (base form)
⚑ Critical Rules — Never Forget
  • V5 is ONLY used in simple present affirmative with he/she/it
  • ❌ "does not plays" → ✅ "does not play" (V1 after does not)
  • ❌ "did not went" → ✅ "did not go" (V1 after did not)
  • Inversion is compulsory in questions — not optional
  • To be (am/is/are): "not" goes directly after the verb
  • Regular V2 = V3 = base + -ed. Irregular must be memorised.
