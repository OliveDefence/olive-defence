E16 — Voice
Active & Passive · All Tenses · Modals · Imperatives · Questions · Special Structures · Verbs That Cannot Be Passive · Error Spotting — CDS · NDA · AFCAT
CDS NDA AFCAT 6 Sections 15 Questions

Voice shows whether the subject performs the action (active) or receives the action (passive). In CDS/NDA/AFCAT, voice questions appear as fill-in-the-blank conversions, error spotting, and sentence improvement. Key test areas: correct passive formation per tense, verbs that cannot be made passive, pronoun changes, retention of "by" phrase, and special structures (imperatives, modals, questions, double-object verbs).

Section 1 — Core Concept & Formula
⚙️
The Passive Formula
Subject · Object · Agent · be + V3 — the four moving parts
▾
The Universal Passive Formula

Active  →  Subject + Verb + Object
Passive → Object (becomes new subject) + appropriate form of "be" + V3 (past participle) + by + old subject (agent — optional)

The tense of the original verb determines which form of "be" to use. The main verb always becomes V3.

StepWhat ChangesRule
1. Object → SubjectThe object of the active sentence becomes the grammatical subject of the passive sentence"The officer signed the orders." → "The orders were signed…"
2. Verb → be + V3The active verb is replaced by the appropriate tense of "be" + past participle (V3)signed (Simple Past) → were signed
3. Subject → by + AgentThe active subject becomes "by + agent" — placed after the verb. It may be omitted if unknown/unimportant."…by the officer." (or omitted)
4. Pronoun CaseSubject pronouns become object pronouns in the "by" phrase; object pronouns become subject pronounsHe → by him; She → by her; They → by them; Him → He; Her → She; Them → They
Pronoun Changes — Active to Passive
  • I → by me  |  me → I
  • We → by us  |  us → we
  • He → by him  |  him → he
  • She → by her  |  her → she
  • They → by them  |  them → they
  • You → by you (unchanged)
  • It / One → by it / one (unchanged)
⚑ "By" Phrase — When to Keep, When to Drop
  • Keep "by" phrase when the agent is important, specific, or named: "The treaty was signed by the two generals."
  • Drop "by" phrase when: (a) agent is unknown — "The window was broken." (b) agent is obvious — "The criminal was arrested." (c) agent is vague — "It is said that…" / "People were told that…"
  • Avoid "by them / by people / by someone" — these add nothing and are considered poor style in exam answers.
  • Prepositions other than "by" are used with: known to ("He is known to all."), pleased with, surprised at, satisfied with, obliged to, annoyed with/at, astonished at, interested in, made of/from/into.
Section 2 — Verbs That Cannot Be Made Passive
🚫
Intransitive Verbs — No Passive Possible
No object = no passive · The most tested error category
▾

Passive voice requires a transitive verb — one that takes a direct object. That object becomes the new subject. If there is no object, there is nothing to promote to subject position, so passive formation is impossible.

Cannot Be Made Passive — Common Exam Traps
  • ✗ "The accident was occurred." → ✓ "The accident occurred." (occur = intransitive)
  • ✗ "The soldier was died bravely." → ✓ "The soldier died bravely." (die = intransitive)
  • ✗ "The convoy was arrived at dawn." → ✓ "The convoy arrived." (arrive = intransitive)
  • ✗ "He was gone to the front." → ✓ "He went to the front." (go = intransitive)
  • ✗ "The sun was risen." → ✓ "The sun rose." (rise = intransitive)
  • ✗ "The event was happened yesterday." → ✓ "The event happened yesterday." (happen = intransitive)
  • ✗ "He was slept soundly." → ✓ "He slept soundly." (sleep = intransitive)
  • ✗ "She was cried." → ✓ "She cried." (cry [without object] = intransitive)
Key Intransitive Verbs — Never Passive
  • occur, happen, take place, arise, appear, disappear
  • die, fall, rise, go, come, arrive, depart, return
  • sleep, rest, sit, stand, lie (recline), exist, remain
  • laugh, cry, smile, cough, sneeze, swim, run (absolute use)
  • consist of, belong to (prepositional verbs — no passive)
⚑ Verbs That Look Transitive But Aren't — Special Cases
  • resemble: "He resembles his father." → ✗ "His father is resembled by him." — never passive
  • have (= possess): "She has a rifle." → ✗ "A rifle is had by her." — possession sense never passive
  • lack: "He lacks discipline." → ✗ "Discipline is lacked by him." — not used in passive
  • suit / fit (stative): "This uniform suits him." → passive is possible but unusual; not tested
  • contain: "The box contains ammunition." → passive is grammatically possible but stylistically avoided
Section 3 — Subject-Verb Agreement in Passive
⚖️
Passive "be" Must Agree with the New Subject
Singular object → singular "be" · Plural object → plural "be"
▾

When the active object becomes the passive subject, the form of "be" must agree with this new subject — not with the original active subject.

ActivePassiveAgreement Note
"He wrote the report.""The report was written by him."report = singular → was ✓
"He wrote the reports.""The reports were written by him."reports = plural → were ✓
"She reads the order.""The order is read by her."order = singular → is ✓
"They are issuing the orders.""The orders are being issued by them."orders = plural → are ✓
"He has submitted the file.""The file has been submitted by him."file = singular → has ✓
"They had completed the tasks.""The tasks had been completed by them."tasks = plural → had ✓
Agreement Error — Most Common in Exams
  • ✗ "The orders was signed by the general." → ✓ "The orders were signed…" (orders = plural)
  • ✗ "The soldiers has been deployed." → ✓ "The soldiers have been deployed." (soldiers = plural)
  • ✗ "Many cadets was selected." → ✓ "Many cadets were selected."

known to · pleased with · surprised at · made of — exam vocabulary

Section 5 — Double Object Verbs in Passive
🔀
Ditransitive Verbs — Two Passive Options
give · send · offer · show · teach · tell · award · pay
▾

When an active sentence has both a direct object (DO) and an indirect object (IO), either object can become the subject of the passive sentence — giving two valid passive forms. The more natural and common form promotes the indirect object.

ActivePassive — IO as Subject (preferred)Passive — DO as Subject
"The general gave the soldiers [IO] their orders [DO].""The soldiers were given their orders by the general." ✓ (more natural)"Their orders were given to the soldiers by the general." ✓
"She sent him [IO] a message [DO].""He was sent a message by her." ✓"A message was sent to him by her." ✓
"They offered the cadet [IO] a scholarship [DO].""The cadet was offered a scholarship." ✓"A scholarship was offered to the cadet." ✓
"He showed us [IO] the map [DO].""We were shown the map by him." ✓"The map was shown to us by him." ✓
"The academy awarded him [IO] a medal [DO].""He was awarded a medal." ✓"A medal was awarded to him." ✓
Which Passive to Choose in Exams?

Both forms are grammatically correct. However, in CDS/NDA/AFCAT fill-in-the-blank questions, the answer choices will make only one form correct based on what the new subject is. Always check: what is the new subject of the passive sentence? Match "be" to that subject. When both are offered as options, prefer the IO as subject form — it is the more idiomatic and commonly used structure.

Section 6 — Passive with "It is said / known / believed"
💬
Impersonal Passive — "It is / He is said to…"
Reporting verbs · Two passive patterns · Highly tested in CDS
▾

Verbs like say, think, believe, know, consider, report, suppose, allege, claim, understand, expect can form a special passive in two ways — both are correct and tested.

Active (informal)Pattern 1: "It is said that…"Pattern 2: "He is said to…"
"People say that he is brave.""It is said that he is brave." ✓"He is said to be brave." ✓
"People think that she has won.""It is thought that she has won." ✓"She is thought to have won." ✓
"They believe that he had left.""It is believed that he had left." ✓"He is believed to have left." ✓
"People know he is honest.""It is known that he is honest." ✓"He is known to be honest." ✓
"They expect her to win.""It is expected that she will win." ✓"She is expected to win." ✓
"They alleged he had cheated.""It was alleged that he had cheated." ✓"He was alleged to have cheated." ✓
Tense in Pattern 2 — Infinitive Choice
  • If the subordinate verb is same time or future → use to + base: "He is said to be brave." (present = present)
  • If the subordinate verb is before main verb time (past reference) → use to have + V3: "He is said to have been brave." (past action reported now)
  • ✗ "He is said to had been brave." → ✓ "He is said to have been brave."

Every tense has a specific passive formula. The core rule: replace the active verb with the correct form of "be" + V3. The tense of the active sentence determines which form of "be" to use. Learn the formula for each tense — this is the most direct way to ace voice conversion questions.

Simple Tenses
Simple Present Most Tested
Active Formula
S + V₁(s/es) + O
The officer signs the order.
Passive Formula
O + is/am/are + V3 + (by S)
The order is signed by the officer.
Simple Past Most Tested
Active Formula
S + V₂ + O
The general commanded the troops.
Passive Formula
O + was/were + V3 + (by S)
The troops were commanded by the general.
Simple Future
Active Formula
S + will + V₁ + O
They will deploy the regiment.
Passive Formula
O + will be + V3 + (by S)
The regiment will be deployed by them.
Progressive (Continuous) Tenses
Present Progressive Common Error
Active Formula
S + is/am/are + V-ing + O
The army is planning an operation.
Passive Formula
O + is/am/are + being + V3 + (by S)
An operation is being planned by the army.
Past Progressive Common Error
Active Formula
S + was/were + V-ing + O
The unit was constructing the bridge.
Passive Formula
O + was/were + being + V3 + (by S)
The bridge was being constructed by the unit.
Future Progressive Rarely Used in Passive
Active Formula
S + will be + V-ing + O
The team will be reviewing the plans.
Passive Formula
O + will be being + V3 (uncommon)
The plans will be being reviewed. (rarely used — prefer: will be reviewed)
Perfect Tenses
Present Perfect Most Tested
Active Formula
S + has/have + V3 + O
She has completed the mission.
Passive Formula
O + has/have + been + V3 + (by S)
The mission has been completed by her.
Past Perfect Most Tested
Active Formula
S + had + V3 + O
He had issued the orders before dawn.
Passive Formula
O + had + been + V3 + (by S)
The orders had been issued before dawn by him.
Future Perfect
Active Formula
S + will have + V3 + O
They will have finished the exercise.
Passive Formula
O + will have + been + V3 + (by S)
The exercise will have been finished by them.
Perfect Progressive Tenses
Present Perfect Progressive Generally Avoided in Passive
Active Formula
S + has/have + been + V-ing + O
They have been monitoring the border.
Passive Formula
O + has/have + been + being + V3 (rarely used)
The border has been being monitored. (awkward — avoid in formal use; use present perfect passive instead)
Past Perfect Progressive Generally Avoided in Passive
Active Formula
S + had + been + V-ing + O
She had been drafting the report for hours.
Passive Formula
O + had + been + being + V3 (rarely used)
The report had been being drafted for hours. (uncommon — prefer past perfect passive)
Modal Tenses
Modal (Present / Future) Most Tested
Active Formula
S + modal + V₁ + O
You must obey the orders.  /  She can complete the task.
Passive Formula
O + modal + be + V3 + (by S)
The orders must be obeyed.  /  The task can be completed by her.
Perfect Modal (Past Reference) Most Tested
Active Formula
S + modal + have + V3 + O
He should have submitted the report.
Passive Formula
O + modal + have been + V3 + (by S)
The report should have been submitted by him.

Beyond simple tense conversions, CDS/NDA/AFCAT papers test special sentence types — imperatives, questions, sentences with "Let", sentences beginning with "It is time / It is necessary", and two-verb structures. Each has its own passive pattern.

Imperative Sentences (Commands)
🎖️
Passive of Imperative (Command) Sentences
Let + O + be + V3 · Negative: Let + O + not + be + V3
▾
Imperative Passive Formula

Active imperative: V₁ + Object (subject "you" is implied)
Passive imperative: Let + Object + be + V3

Active (Command)Passive
"Open the door.""Let the door be opened."
"Clean the rifle.""Let the rifle be cleaned."
"Submit the report immediately.""Let the report be submitted immediately."
"Do not touch the equipment.""Let the equipment not be touched."
"Do not waste time.""Let time not be wasted."
"Follow the orders strictly.""Let the orders be followed strictly."
"Help the wounded soldier.""Let the wounded soldier be helped."
"Post the letter.""Let the letter be posted."
⚑ Alternative Imperative Passive Forms
  • Some grammar books also accept: "You are requested / ordered / advised to…" when the command is a request or instruction to a specific person.
  • "Please open the window." → "You are requested to open the window." ✓ (polite request form)
  • "Attend the parade." → "You are ordered to attend the parade." ✓ (command form)
  • For CDS/NDA/AFCAT, both the Let O be V3 form and the You are requested/ordered/advised to + V1 form are accepted — check the answer options provided.
Interrogative Sentences (Questions)
❓
Passive of Questions
Yes/No questions · Wh-questions · Inversion maintained
▾
Question Passive Rule

The passive of a question retains the question form — the auxiliary verb stays inverted before the subject. The object of the active question becomes the subject of the passive question.

Active QuestionPassive Question
"Did he write the letter?""Was the letter written by him?"
"Has she completed the report?""Has the report been completed by her?"
"Will they deploy the regiment?""Will the regiment be deployed by them?"
"Can you finish the task?""Can the task be finished by you?"
"Who wrote this order?""By whom was this order written?" ✓ (formal)
OR "Who was this order written by?" (informal)
"Whom did he appoint?""Who was appointed by him?"
"What did the CO order?""What was ordered by the CO?"
⚑ "Who" in Questions → "By Whom" in Passive
  • "Who attacked the base?" → "By whom was the base attacked?" ✓ (formal exam form)
  • In the active, "who" = subject. In the passive, the original subject becomes the agent: "by whom".
  • "Whom did he select?" → "Who was selected by him?" — "whom" (object) becomes "who" (subject) ✓
Sentences with "Let" (Proposals / Suggestions)
💡
Passive of "Let" Sentences
Let us / Let him / Let them → Let O be V3
▾
Active ("Let" sentence)Passive
"Let us do this work.""Let this work be done by us." ✓
"Let him complete the task.""Let the task be completed by him." ✓
"Let them prepare the report.""Let the report be prepared by them." ✓
"Let her sign the papers.""Let the papers be signed by her." ✓
⚑ "Let us" vs Imperative "Let" — Different Passive Forms
  • "Let us do it" (proposal/suggestion) → "Let it be done by us." ✓
  • "Let the door be opened" (imperative passive — from "Open the door") — this IS already in the passive form
  • Do not confuse the two: one starts from a "let us/him/her/them + V" structure; the other is the standard imperative passive using "Let O be V3".
Two-Verb Structures & Other Special Forms
⚡
Two Verbs, "Need to", "Have to", "Used to" in Passive
It is time · It is necessary · have to · need to · ought to
▾
ActivePassiveNote
"He has to submit the form.""The form has to be submitted by him."has to → has to be + V3
"They need to repair the vehicle.""The vehicle needs to be repaired by them."need to → need/needs to be + V3
"She ought to file the report.""The report ought to be filed by her."ought to → ought to be + V3
"He used to clean the weapons.""The weapons used to be cleaned by him."used to → used to be + V3
"It is time to sound the alarm.""It is time for the alarm to be sounded."to + V1 → to be + V3
"It is necessary to check the list.""It is necessary for the list to be checked."same pattern
"He wants to see the results.""He wants the results to be seen." / "The results want to be seen by him." (less natural)prefer: wants the O to be V3
Causative Passive — Have / Get + Object + V3
  • Have/Get + Object + V3 is the causative construction — the subject arranges for someone else to do the action. This is already passive-like in meaning.
  • "He had his rifle cleaned." (= He arranged for someone to clean it) ✓
  • "She got her uniform pressed." ✓
  • "The CO had the report reviewed by the adjutant." ✓
  • Note: "have + object + V3" and "get + object + V3" are distinct from "have + been + V3" (passive perfect).
Perception / Sense Verbs — Special Passive
👁️
See / Hear / Feel / Watch / Notice in Passive
Bare infinitive → to-infinitive in passive; -ing stays -ing
▾

Perception verbs take an object + bare infinitive (or -ing) in the active. In the passive, the bare infinitive becomes a to-infinitive; the -ing form stays unchanged.

ActivePassive
"We saw him cross the bridge." (bare inf.)"He was seen to cross the bridge by us." ✓
"They heard her sing." (bare inf.)"She was heard to sing by them." ✓
"We saw him crossing the bridge." (-ing)"He was seen crossing the bridge by us." ✓ (-ing kept)
"They heard her singing." (-ing)"She was heard singing by them." ✓ (-ing kept)
"I noticed him leave early." (bare inf.)"He was noticed to leave early by me." ✓
"We watched them practise." (bare inf.)"They were watched to practise / practising by us." ✓
Perception Verb Passive — Common Exam Error
  • ✗ "He was seen cross the bridge." → ✓ "He was seen to cross the bridge." (bare inf. → to-inf. in passive)
  • ✗ "She was heard sing." → ✓ "She was heard to sing." ✓
Quick Reference — All Passive Formulas at a Glance
Simple Tenses — Passive "Be" Form
  • Simple Present: is / am / are + V3
  • Simple Past: was / were + V3
  • Simple Future: will be + V3
  • Future (going to): is/am/are going to be + V3
Progressive Tenses — Passive "Be" Form
  • Present Progressive: is/am/are + being + V3
  • Past Progressive: was/were + being + V3
  • Future Progressive: will be being + V3 (very rare)
  • Key: progressive passive uses being between aux and V3
Perfect Tenses — Passive "Be" Form
  • Present Perfect: has/have + been + V3
  • Past Perfect: had + been + V3
  • Future Perfect: will have + been + V3
  • Key: perfect passive uses been between aux and V3
Modal Passives
  • Present modal: modal + be + V3
  • Perfect modal: modal + have been + V3
  • can → can be / could have been
  • must → must be / must have been
  • should → should be / should have been
Special Sentence Passives
  • Imperative: "Clean it." → "Let it be cleaned."
  • Neg. Imperative: "Don't waste it." → "Let it not be wasted."
  • Let us: "Let us do it." → "Let it be done by us."
  • Questions: Retain inversion; "Who?" → "By whom?"
  • Perception verbs: bare inf → to-inf in passive; -ing stays
Verbs That Cannot Be Passive
  • All intransitive verbs: occur, happen, die, arrive, go, rise, fall, sleep, come, exist
  • ✗ "was occurred / was died / was arrived"
  • resemble, have (possess), lack — no passive even though transitive
  • Test: If there is no direct object, there is no passive.
Prepositions Other Than "By"
  • known to / pleased/satisfied with
  • surprised/astonished at / interested in
  • made of (visible) / made from (changed)
  • filled/covered with / crowded with
  • tired of / annoyed with (person) / at (thing)
  • obliged to / engaged in
Pronoun Changes (Active → Passive)
  • I → by me  |  me → I (subject)
  • We → by us  |  us → We
  • He → by him  |  him → He
  • She → by her  |  her → She
  • They → by them  |  them → They
  • You → by you (unchanged)
