Look around you. The snow in Gulmarg is white. The apple from Sopore is red and sweet. The Dal Lake is large and beautiful. The words in bold are adjectives — they describe the things around us.

An adjective is a word that describes a noun. It tells us what kind, how many, which one, or what colour the noun is. Without adjectives, language would be dull and vague.

Part 1 — What Is an Adjective?
🎨
Adjectives — Words That Describe Nouns
An adjective adds detail to a noun — it answers "what kind?", "how many?", etc.
▾

An adjective modifies a noun — it sharpens our picture of the noun. It answers one of these questions:

→ Four Questions an Adjective Answers
  • What kind? — a tall mountain · a sweet mango · a cold wind
  • How many? — three apples · many students · few birds
  • Which one? — this book · that house · the first seat
  • What colour? — a red flower · the blue sky · a white shawl
🔮 The Camera Analogy

Imagine describing a photo to someone who cannot see it. "There is a tall, dark-haired boy near a small, wooden boat on a calm, blue lake." Every bold word is an adjective. Adjectives are your zoom lens — they bring the picture into focus.

Spot the Adjective
  • The fluffy clouds floated over the green valley. — 2 adjectives
  • Arjun is a clever and hardworking student. — 2 adjectives
  • She wore a long, embroidered phiran on a cold morning.
  • The old bridge near the small village was beautiful.

Can a Noun Have More Than One Adjective?

Yes! When several adjectives describe one noun, they follow a natural order:
Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Colour → Material → Noun
e.g. "a beautiful tall old round golden clock"

Adjective comes after the linking verb and still describes the subject (noun).
→ Common Linking Verbs
  • To be: is · am · are · was · were (most common)
  • Sensory: looks · sounds · feels · smells · tastes
  • Other: seems · becomes · appears · remains · stays
  • After ALL these verbs → use an adjective (not an adverb!)
⚑ Adjective vs Adverb — The Classic Mix-up
  • Adjectives describe nouns: "She is a quick learner." → quick describes learner
  • Adverbs describe verbs: "She learns quickly." → quickly describes learns
  • ❌ "She sings beautiful." → ✅ "She sings beautifully." (adverb after verb)
  • ❌ "The flower smells sweetly." → ✅ "The flower smells sweet." (adjective after linking verb)
Part 4 — Identifying Adjectives
🔍
Three Quick Tests to Find an Adjective
Use these every time you are unsure whether a word is an adjective
▾
★ The Three Tests
  • Test 1 — The "What kind?" question: If the word answers "What kind of noun?", it is an adjective. "A cold day." → What kind of day? Cold ✅
  • Test 2 — The "very" test: If you can put "very" before it and it makes sense, it is usually an adjective. "very tall" ✅ · "very run" ❌ (run = verb)
  • Test 3 — Position test: It appears directly before a noun, or after a linking verb. "beautiful flower" ✅ · "The flower is beautiful" ✅
Practice — Find All Adjectives
  • "The old, tired camel sat near the green pond." → 3 adjectives
  • "Rohan is clever but also very lazy." → 2 adjectives
  • "She picked five ripe mangoes from the tall tree." → 3 adjectives
  • "The cold, dark night felt very long." → 3 adjectives
Part 5 — An Introduction to Degrees
📊
Comparing Things — Three Degrees
Positive · Comparative · Superlative
▾

We often use adjectives to compare people, places, and things. English has three degrees of comparison.

DegreeWhat It MeansThings ComparedExample
PositiveJust describing — no comparison1 thingArjun is tall.
ComparativeOne has MORE of the quality2 thingsArjun is taller than Rohan.
SuperlativeOne has the MOST in a group3+ thingsArjun is the tallest in the class.
🏔️ The Mountain Analogy

Three mountains near Srinagar. One is high — Positive (just describing). A second is higher than the first — Comparative (comparing two). The third is the highest of all — Superlative (best of the whole group).

Key Signal Words — Never Forget These

Comparative → always followed by than: "taller than", "more beautiful than"
Superlative → always preceded by the: "the tallest", "the most beautiful"

Three rules govern how degrees are formed. Which rule to use depends on the length of the adjective and whether it is regular or irregular. Learn the rules, then use the searchable table below to look up any adjective.

The Three Formation Rules
Rule 1 — Short Adjectives (1 syllable · most 2-syllable adjectives)
Positive → Positive + -er (Comparative) → the + Positive + -est (Superlative)
tall → taller → the tallest
cold → colder → the coldest
smart → smarter → the smartest
Spelling Patterns for -er / -est

Ends in silent -e: drop the e, add -r / -st → nice → nicer → nicest
CVC (consonant-vowel-consonant): double final consonant → big → bigger · hot → hotter
Ends in -y (after consonant): y → i, then -er / -est → happy → happier → happiest

Rule 2 — Long Adjectives (3+ syllables · many 2-syllable -ful/-ous/-ing/-ed adjectives)
Positive → more + Positive (Comparative) → the most + Positive (Superlative)
beautiful → more beautiful → the most beautiful
important → more important → the most important
exciting → more exciting → the most exciting
⚑ Never Double-Form
  • ❌ "more taller" / "more tallest" — never add more/most to a short adjective
  • ❌ "beautifuler" — never add -er/-est to a long adjective
  • Quick rule: 1–2 syllables → Rule 1 · 3+ syllables → Rule 2
Rule 3 — Irregular Adjectives (must be memorised — no rule applies!)
Positive → Completely Different Word (Comparative) → Completely Different Word (Superlative)
good → better → the best
bad → worse → the worst
many / much → more → the most
little → less → the least
far → farther / further → the farthest / furthest

🎨 What Is an Adjective?
  • A word that describes a noun (person, place, animal, thing)
  • Answers: What kind? How many? Which one? What colour?
  • very test: "very tall" ✅ → adjective · "very run" ❌ → not adjective
  • Examples: tall, red, five, round, happy, beautiful, cold
📦 Six Types of Adjectives
  • Quality: kind, brave, clean, honest, beautiful
  • Size: big, small, tall, short, tiny, huge, wide
  • Colour: red, blue, green, golden, white, black
  • Number/Quantity: one, many, few, all, some, several
  • Shape: round, square, flat, oval, triangular
  • Feeling/Opinion: happy, sad, wonderful, terrible
📍 Two Positions of Adjectives
  • Attributive (before noun): a tall boy · a cold wind
  • Predicative (after linking verb): The boy is tall. The wind was cold.
  • Linking verbs: is/am/are/was/were · looks · seems · feels · sounds · smells · tastes
  • After a verb of action → need adverb, not adjective: runs quickly
📊 Three Degrees of Comparison
  • Positive: just describing — "tall"
  • Comparative: 2 things, use than → "taller than"
  • Superlative: 3+ things, use the → "the tallest"
  • Comparative always needs than · Superlative always needs the
📐 Formation Rules
  • Short (1–2 syllable): + -er / -est → cold → colder → coldest
  • Ends in -e: + -r / -st → nice → nicer → nicest
  • CVC pattern: double final consonant → big → bigger → biggest
  • Ends in -y: y→i + er/est → happy → happier → happiest
  • Long (3+ syllable): more / the most + adj → more beautiful
  • ❌ Never: "more taller" · "beautifuler" · "more tallest"
⚠️ Irregular Degrees — Memorise These
  • good → better → the best
  • bad → worse → the worst
  • many / much → more → the most
  • little (quantity) → less → the least
  • far → farther / further → the farthest / furthest
  • old (family) → elder → the eldest
⚑ Critical Rules — Never Forget
  • Comparative + than: "taller than" — the "than" is compulsory
  • Superlative + the: "the tallest" — the "the" is compulsory
  • ❌ "more taller" → ✅ "taller" (short adj: only -er)
  • ❌ "beautifuler" → ✅ "more beautiful" (long adj: only more)
  • ❌ "She sings beautiful." → ✅ "She sings beautifully."
  • ❌ "more better" / "more best" — good/better/best: never add more/most
