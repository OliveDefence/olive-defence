Determiners —
Much, Many, Few & More

Determiners — the words that come before nouns to specify quantity, reference and identity — are tested heavily in CDS, NDA and AFCAT Fill in the Blanks and Spotting Errors. The confusion between much/many, few/little, each/every, and other pairs trips up most candidates. This module fixes that.

Exam Focused CDS · NDA · AFCAT 20 Practice Questions

A determiner is a word placed before a noun to express reference, quantity, or identification. Unlike adjectives, determiners do not describe a noun — they identify or quantify it. The six categories tested in CDS/NDA/AFCAT are: articles (covered separately), quantifiers (much/many, few/little, some/any), distributives (each/every, either/neither), possessives (my, your, his…), demonstratives (this/that, these/those), and numbers & ordinals. This module focuses on the categories most tested at medium–high level.

Section 1 — Quantifiers: Much, Many, More, Most
⚖️
Much vs Many
Countable vs uncountable — the foundational split
▾

This is the most fundamental quantifier distinction. The rule never changes: much is used with uncountable nouns; many is used with countable nouns.

DeterminerUsed withExamples
MuchUncountable nounsmuch water, much time, much courage, much information
ManyCountable plural nounsmany soldiers, many questions, many officers, many days
Correct Usage
  • He showed much courage during the mission. ✓
  • Many cadets applied for the NDA examination. ✓
  • There is not much time left for the briefing. ✓
Classic Errors
  • He has many experience in mountain warfare. → much experience ✓
  • There is much soldiers in the platoon. → many soldiers ✓
  • She gave many advice to the recruits. → much advice ✓
⚑ Uncountable Nouns That Fool Candidates
  • much: advice, information, knowledge, furniture, equipment, luggage, scenery, traffic, progress, research, work, news, weather, evidence
  • These all look like they could be countable but are not. Never use "many" with them.
📊
More, Most, Less, Least, Fewer, Fewest
Comparative and superlative quantifiers — and the fewer/less trap
▾
FormCountableUncountable
Comparative "more"more soldiersmore water

Fewer vs Less — The Key Rule

Fewer is used with countable nouns (things you can count individually).
Less is used with uncountable nouns (things measured in bulk/quantity).

"There are fewer vacancies this year." (vacancies = countable) ✓
"There is less rainfall this monsoon." (rainfall = uncountable) ✓

Errors
  • There are less candidates this year. → fewer candidates ✓
  • We had fewer time to prepare. → less time ✓
Section 2 — Few, Little, A Few, A Little
🔢
Few / A Few / The Few & Little / A Little / The Little
The article changes the entire meaning — the most tested distinction
▾

This is one of the most frequently tested determiner rules in CDS/NDA/AFCAT. The presence or absence of "a" completely changes the meaning — from negative to positive.

FormNoun typeMeaningTone
FewCountable pluralHardly any — almost noneNegative
A fewCountable pluralSome — not many, but enoughPositive
The fewCountable pluralThe small number that exist (all of them)Specific
LittleUncountableHardly any — almost noneNegative
A littleUncountableSome — not much, but enoughPositive
The littleUncountableWhatever small amount exists (all of it)Specific
Meaning in Context
  • "He has few friends in the regiment." → He is almost friendless. (sad) ✓
  • "He has a few friends in the regiment." → He has some friends. (fine) ✓
  • "There is little hope of rescue." → Almost no hope. (grim) ✓
  • "There is a little hope of rescue." → Some hope remains. (reassuring) ✓
  • "Spend the little money you have wisely." → Use all of the small amount. ✓
⚑ Choosing Based on Context

In fill-in-the-blank questions, read the tone of the sentence. If the sentence implies hardship, scarcity, or pessimism → use few / little. If the sentence implies sufficiency or hope → use a few / a little. This tonal clue is always present.

Section 3 — Some, Any, No & Their Compounds
🔀
Some vs Any
Affirmative vs negative/interrogative — and the exceptions
▾
DeterminerUsed inExample
SomeAffirmative sentencesThere are some officers in the hall.
SomeQuestions expecting "yes"Could I have some water, please?
SomeOffers & requestsWould you like some tea?
AnyNegative sentencesThere aren't any vacancies.
AnyGenuine questions (yes/no unclear)Are there any messages for me?
AnyConditional clauses (if/whether)If any reinforcements arrive, inform me.
Compound Forms
  • some-: someone, somebody, something, somewhere → affirmative contexts
  • any-: anyone, anybody, anything, anywhere → negative/question/conditional
  • no-: no one, nobody, nothing, nowhere → negative meaning, affirmative structure
  • "I didn't meet anybody." = "I met nobody." (same meaning, different structure)
⚑ "Any" in Affirmative = "Every" / "It doesn't matter which"

"Any officer can confirm this." → Here "any" does not mean a question or negative — it means every / whichever. This special affirmative use of "any" is tested in CDS/NDA to distinguish it from "some".

Section 4 — Distributive Determiners
🎯
Each vs Every
Both mean "all individually" — but with key grammatical differences
▾

Both each and every refer to individual members of a group and both take a singular noun and singular verb. However, they are not always interchangeable.

FeatureEachEvery
Minimum numberTwo or moreThree or more (implies a larger group)
FocusIndividual members separatelyAll members together as a totality
PositionCan follow subject: "They each received…"Always before noun: "Every soldier received…"
Used aloneYes — "Give each a turn."No — needs a noun: ✗ "Give every a turn."
With "not"Each can deny individuallyEvery + not = partial denial (not all)
Correct Usage
  • Each of the two cadets received a medal. ✓ (only two)
  • Every cadet in the battalion reported on time. ✓ (large group)
  • Not every plan succeeds. ✓ (= some don't)
⚑ "Every other" is Correct — "Each other" is Different
  • "Every other day" = alternate days → uses "every", not "each"
  • "Each other" = reciprocal pronoun (they helped each other) → fixed phrase, not replaceable with "every"
⚡
Either vs Neither vs Both
Always used for exactly two — and the agreement rules
▾
DeterminerMeaningVerbExample
EitherOne or the other of twoSingularEither route leads to the camp.
NeitherNot one, not the otherSingularNeither officer was available.
BothThe two togetherPluralBoth soldiers were decorated.
Errors
  • Either of the three routes is safe. → Any of the three routes ✓ (either = only two)
  • Neither of the three officers arrived. → None of the three officers ✓
  • Both the soldiers was brave. → Both the soldiers were brave ✓
The "Two Only" Rule

Either, neither, and both are strictly for two items. When three or more are involved: use any instead of either, none instead of neither, and all instead of both.

Section 5 — Demonstratives & Other Determiners
👆
This / That / These / Those
Proximity and number — and the "these kind" trap
▾
Near (proximity)Far (distance)
Singularthisthat
Pluralthesethose
Agreement: Demonstrative Must Match Noun in Number
  • This / That + singular noun: this book, that officer
  • These / Those + plural noun: these books, those soldiers
  • Never: these kind of people ✗ → this kind of people OR these kinds of people ✓
  • Never: those sort of problems ✗ → that sort of problem OR those sorts of problems ✓
⚑ "Such" as a Determiner
  • "Such a brave soldier" — singular countable: such + a/an + adj + noun
  • "Such brave soldiers" — plural: such + noun (no article)
  • "Such courage" — uncountable: such + noun (no article)
  • Never: such a courage ✗ (courage is uncountable)
🔑
Enough, Plenty of, Sufficient & All / Whole
Position, usage and the "all vs whole" distinction
▾
"Enough" — Position Rule

As a determiner (before a noun): enough comes before the noun.
As an adverb (after an adjective/adverb): enough comes after the adjective.

"There is enough food for the platoon." (determiner before noun) ✓
"He is experienced enough for the mission." (adverb after adjective) ✓
✗ "He is enough experienced…" — wrong word order

DeterminerRuleExample
All + theAll the + plural or uncountable nounAll the soldiers were present.
WholeThe whole + singular countable nounThe whole battalion was deployed.
All + day/night/weekNo article: all day, all nightHe marched all day. ✓
All vs Whole — Errors
  • All the battalion was deployed. → The whole battalion ✓ (singular countable)
  • The whole soldiers were present. → All the soldiers ✓ (plural)
  • The whole milk was spilled. → All the milk ✓ (uncountable)
Section 6 — Order of Determiners
📋
When Multiple Determiners Appear Together
Pre-determiners, central determiners, and post-determiners
▾

When a noun phrase contains more than one determiner, they must appear in a fixed order.

The Fixed Order

Pre-determiner → Central determiner → Post-determiner → Noun

Pre-determiners: all, both, half, double, twice, such
Central determiners: articles (a/an/the), possessives (my/his…), demonstratives (this/that), quantifiers (some/any/each/every/either/neither/no)
Post-determiners: ordinals (first, second…), cardinals (one, two…), general ordinals (next, last, other)

Correct Order Examples
  • All [pre] + the [central] + five [post] soldiers → "All the five soldiers" ✓
  • Both [pre] + his [central] + two [post] sons → "Both his two sons" ✓
  • Half [pre] + a [central] + hour → "Half an hour" ✓
Wrong Order Errors
  • The all five soldiers → All the five soldiers ✓
  • His both sons → Both his sons ✓
  • My these books → These books of mine ✓ (possessive + demonstrative cannot co-occur directly)
Determiners — Complete Quick Reference
Much / Many / Fewer / Less
  • Much + uncountable: much water, much advice
  • Many + countable plural: many soldiers, many days
  • Fewer + countable plural: fewer candidates
  • Less + uncountable: less time, less water
  • Never: less candidates ✗ or fewer time ✗
  • Tricky uncountables: advice, information, furniture, equipment, luggage, work, news
Few / A Few / Little / A Little
  • Few (countable) = hardly any → negative tone
  • A few (countable) = some → positive tone
  • The few = all of the small number
  • Little (uncountable) = hardly any → negative tone
  • A little (uncountable) = some → positive tone
  • Tone clue: pessimism/scarcity → few/little; sufficiency → a few/a little
Each / Every / Either / Neither / Both
  • Each: 2 or more, individual focus, can stand alone
  • Every: 3 or more, collective/total focus, always + noun
  • Either / Neither / Both: strictly for two items only
  • 3+ items → use any (not either), none (not neither), all (not both)
  • Every other day = alternate days (fixed phrase)
  • Each other = reciprocal pronoun (cannot use "every other" here)
Some / Any / No
  • Some: affirmative sentences; offers/requests; questions expecting yes
  • Any: negative sentences; genuine yes/no questions; conditional clauses
  • Any (affirmative) = every / whichever: Any soldier can do this.
  • No: replaces "not any" — affirmative structure, negative meaning
  • I saw nobody = I didn't see anybody (not: I didn't see nobody)
All vs Whole / Enough / Such
  • All the + plural/uncountable: all the soldiers, all the milk
  • The whole + singular countable: the whole battalion
  • Enough before noun (det.) / after adjective (adv.): enough food; brave enough
  • Such a/an + singular countable: such a brave officer
  • Such + plural/uncountable (no article): such soldiers; such courage
Order of Determiners
  • Pre-det.: all, both, half, double, such
  • Central det.: articles, possessives, demonstratives, quantifiers
  • Post-det.: ordinals, cardinals, next/last/other
  • All the five soldiers ✓ — not The all five ✗
  • Both his sons ✓ — not His both sons ✗
  • Demonstrative + possessive cannot co-occur: use "of mine/yours" instead
