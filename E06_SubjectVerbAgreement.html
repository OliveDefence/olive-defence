English Grammar · Subject-Verb Agreement

Subject–Verb
Agreement

SVA errors are embedded in almost every CDS, NDA and AFCAT Spotting Errors paper. Intervening phrases, collective nouns, inverted sentences, tricky connectors — learn the twelve rules that govern every pattern the examiners use.

Medium–High Level CDS · NDA · AFCAT 20 Practice Questions

The golden rule of SVA: the verb must agree in number and person with its subject — not with any intervening noun, not with the complement, and not with the nearest word. The twelve rules below cover every pattern that CDS/NDA/AFCAT examiners use. Each rule has its own "trap" that candidates routinely fall into.

Section 1 — The Core Rules
📌
Rule 1 — Intervening Phrases
Phrases between subject and verb do not change agreement
▾

Phrases introduced by with, along with, together with, as well as, in addition to, including, except, rather than, no less than, accompanied by — when placed between the subject and verb — do not change the number of the verb. The verb still agrees with the original subject.

The Rule

Mentally bracket the intervening phrase and match the verb to the true subject only.
"The general, [along with his officers], was present." → subject = general (singular) → was ✓

Correct Usage
  • The colonel, along with his troops, was decorated. ✓
  • The plan, as well as the execution, was flawed. ✓
  • The captain, together with the crew, has arrived. ✓
Classic Errors
  • The officer, along with his men, were present. → was present ✓
  • The plan, as well as the resources, were ready. → was ready ✓
🔗
Rule 2 — Either/Or and Neither/Nor
Verb agrees with the subject nearer to it
▾

With either…or and neither…nor, the verb agrees with the subject that is closest to it (the nearer subject rule).

SentenceNearer SubjectVerb
Either the soldiers or the captain ___captain (singular)is / was
Either the captain or the soldiers ___soldiers (plural)are / were
Neither he nor I ___I (1st person)am
Neither I nor he ___he (3rd person)is
⚑ Exam Trap — Person Agreement

With neither/nor and either/or, the verb must agree in both number AND person with the nearer subject.
"Neither you nor I am responsible." (I = 1st person → am)
"Neither I nor he is responsible." (he = 3rd person → is)

👥
Rule 3 — Collective Nouns
Army, committee, jury, team — unit vs divided action
▾

A collective noun takes a singular verb when the group acts as a single unit, and a plural verb when members act individually.

Singular (acting as a unit)
  • The jury has reached its verdict. ✓
  • The committee has approved the budget. ✓
  • The army was advancing towards the border. ✓
Plural (acting individually)
  • The jury were divided in their opinions. ✓
  • The committee were arguing among themselves. ✓
Always Singular Collective Nouns (no exception in exams)
  • The public, the press, the clergy, the aristocracy, the gentry → always singular in Indian exam context
  • "The public is requested to maintain silence." ✓
🔢
Rule 4 — Indefinite Pronouns as Subjects
Each, every, either, neither, one, anyone → always singular
▾

Indefinite pronouns that refer to singular entities always take a singular verb — even when followed by a plural phrase.

Always Take Singular Verb
  • each, either, neither, every, one, everyone, everybody, someone, anyone, nobody, nothing, anything
Correct Usage
  • Each of the soldiers was given a medal. ✓
  • Neither of the plans was approved. ✓
  • Everyone in the battalion has reported. ✓
  • One of the officers has resigned. ✓
⚑ "Each" After a Plural Subject

When "each" comes after a plural subject, the verb is plural:
"The soldiers each have their weapons." (subject = soldiers, plural)
But: "Each of the soldiers has a weapon." (subject = each, singular)

Section 2 — Tricky Subject Patterns
📐
Rule 5 — "The number of" vs "A number of"
One of the most tested distinctions in CDS/NDA
▾
The Rule

"The number of" + plural noun → singular verb (refers to a specific count)
"A number of" + plural noun → plural verb (means "several / many")

Correct Usage
  • The number of applicants has increased. ✓ (specific figure)
  • A number of officers have been promoted. ✓ (= several officers)
  • The number of vacancies is twenty. ✓
➕
Rule 6 — Subjects Joined by "And"
Usually plural — but three important exceptions
▾

Two or more subjects joined by "and" normally take a plural verb. However, there are three exceptions that examiners love to test.

ExceptionRuleExample
Same person / thingSingular verbThe poet and soldier was Chekhov. (one person with two roles)
Each/Every before compoundSingular verbEvery man and woman is responsible.
Inseparable pairSingular verbBread and butter is my breakfast. (treated as one thing)
Common Inseparable Pairs → Singular Verb
  • Bread and butter, rice and curry, slow and steady, trial and error
  • Law and order has broken down. ✓
  • Slow and steady wins the race. ✓
🔀
Rule 7 — Inverted Sentences & "There is/are"
When the subject comes after the verb
▾

In sentences beginning with "there" or in inverted constructions, the verb must agree with the real subject that follows it — not with "there".

Correct Usage
  • There is a problem with the plan. (problem = singular) ✓
  • There are three officers in the room. (officers = plural) ✓
  • There was a time when this was different. ✓
⚑ "There is" with Compound Subject

When a compound subject follows "there", the verb agrees with the first (nearest) element:
"There is a pen and two books on the table." (pen = first, singular → is)
"There are two books and a pen on the table." (books = first, plural → are)

Section 3 — Special Noun & Phrase Types
📏
Rule 8 — Plural-Form Nouns that are Singular
News, mathematics, physics, measles — they look plural, act singular
▾

Many nouns end in -s but are grammatically singular in meaning and always take a singular verb.

CategoryExamplesVerb
Academic subjectsmathematics, physics, economics, ethics, politics, statisticssingular
Diseasesmeasles, mumps, rickets, rabiessingular
Abstract/mass nounsnews, innings (cricket — singular), headquarterssingular
Correct Usage
  • The news is disturbing. ✓
  • Mathematics is my favourite subject. ✓
  • Measles is a contagious disease. ✓
  • Politics is a dirty game. ✓
✂️
Rule 9 — Relative Pronoun as Subject
"One of those who…" — a notorious exam trap
▾

When a relative pronoun (who, which, that) is the subject of a clause, the verb in that clause agrees with the antecedent of the relative pronoun.

The "One of those who" Pattern

"He is one of those officers who work tirelessly." → "who" refers to "those officers" (plural) → plural verb "work" ✓

"He is the only one of those officers who works tirelessly." → "the only one" → singular → "works" ✓

With "one of" → plural verb.   With "only one of" → singular verb.

Most Common Error
  • She is one of the students who has topped. → who have topped ✓
  • He is the only one of the pilots who have qualified. → who has qualified ✓
💰
Rule 10 — Expressions of Amount, Distance & Time
When a plural-sounding phrase represents a single quantity
▾

When a phrase expresses a single, unified quantity of money, distance, time, or weight, it takes a singular verb — even if the noun is plural.

Correct Usage — Singular Verb for Unified Quantity
  • Ten kilometres is a long march. ✓ (single distance)
  • Five years is a long time to serve. ✓ (single period)
  • Fifty thousand rupees was the fine. ✓ (single sum)
  • Two thirds of the work has been completed. ✓ (single portion)
⚑ Fraction + of + Noun

With expressions like "half of / a part of / some of / most of / all of + noun", the verb agrees with the noun after "of":
"Half of the soldiers were injured." (soldiers = plural)
"Half of the water was spilled." (water = uncountable/singular)

Section 4 — More High-Frequency Traps
📢
Rule 11 — "Not only…but also" & Other Connectors
The verb follows the subject nearest to it
▾
ConnectorVerb agrees withExample
Not only…but alsoNearer (second) subjectNot only the men but also the captain was tired.
Neither…norNearer (second) subjectNeither the captain nor the men were ready.
Either…orNearer (second) subjectEither the officers or the general is to blame.
With / along withFirst subject onlyThe general, along with his staff, was briefed.
⚡
Rule 12 — Titles, Names & Clauses as Subjects
A clause or title acting as a subject → always singular
▾

When a noun clause, infinitive phrase, gerund phrase, or a title/name acts as the subject, the verb is always singular.

Correct Usage
  • What the soldiers need is better equipment. ✓ (noun clause = subject)
  • To serve the nation is a great honour. ✓ (infinitive phrase)
  • Training young officers requires patience. ✓ (gerund phrase)
  • "The Times" is a reputed newspaper. ✓ (title = singular)
  • "Three Musketeers" is a classic novel. ✓ (title with plural-looking name)
Arithmetical Operations → Singular Verb
  • Two plus two is four. ✓
  • Three times four is twelve. ✓
  • Ten minus six is four. ✓
  • Even division: Twenty divided by five is four. ✓
Subject-Verb Agreement — Complete Quick Reference
Rules 1–3 at a Glance
  • Intervening phrase (with / along with / as well as) → verb agrees with original subject only
  • Either…or / Neither…nor → verb agrees with nearer subject
  • Collective noun as unit → singular verb
  • Collective noun, members individually → plural verb
  • The public / the press / the clergy → always singular in exam context
Indefinite Pronouns → Singular Verb
  • each, either, neither, every, one
  • everyone, everybody, everything
  • someone, somebody, something
  • anyone, anybody, anything
  • no one, nobody, nothing
  • Exception: "The soldiers each have…" — "each" after plural subject → plural
"The number of" vs "A number of"
  • The number of + noun → singular verb
  • The number of applicants has risen.
  • A number of + noun → plural verb (= several)
  • A number of officers have been promoted.
  • Same logic: The majority of → singular; A majority of → plural
Always Singular — Despite -s Ending
  • Subjects: mathematics, physics, economics, statistics, ethics, politics
  • Diseases: measles, mumps, rickets, rabies
  • Others: news, innings, headquarters, whereabouts (can be either)
  • Expressions of unified quantity: Five years is a long time.
  • Arithmetic: Two plus two is four.
"One of those who" vs "Only one of those who"
  • One of those who → plural verb (who = those)
  • He is one of the men who work hard.
  • The only one of those who → singular verb
  • He is the only one who works hard.
  • Fraction/part + of + noun → verb agrees with noun after "of"
  • Half of the water was spilled.
Clauses, Titles & "And" Exceptions
  • Noun clause as subject → singular verb
  • Gerund / infinitive phrase as subject → singular verb
  • Title / book name as subject → singular verb
  • "And" exceptions: same person, each/every before compound, inseparable pair
  • Law and order has broken down. (inseparable pair)
  • Every man and woman is responsible. (every + compound)
