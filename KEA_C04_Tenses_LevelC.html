Tenses — Time & Choice
How time signals (adverbs, phrases, clauses) tell you which tense to use — Present, Past and Future in all their forms. Essential for Level C grammar mastery.
Level C Class 6 Present Past Future Time Signals

The Central Idea: Every sentence has a time signal — a word, phrase, or clause that tells you when the action happens. The tense you choose must match that signal. Get the signal right, and the tense follows automatically.

Think of tense selection like a key-lock system: the time signal is the key, and the correct tense is the lock it opens. In this lesson, every tense is taught through its natural time signals — not just rules to memorise.

Section 1 — Present Tenses & Their Time Signals
🟣
Simple Present
Habits · Routines · Universal truths · Schedules
▾

The Simple Present is used when an action is regular, repeated, or always true. It does NOT describe what is happening right now — it describes what generally happens.

Formula
Subject + V1 (s/es for he/she/it) + Object
Negative: Subject + do/does + not + V1  |  Question: Do/Does + Subject + V1?
Time Signals → Simple Present
  • every day / week / year — "I go to school every day."
  • always usually often sometimes rarely never — frequency adverbs live here
  • in the morning / evening — habitual time of day
  • on Sundays / Mondays — day-of-week routine
  • generally regularly normally
  • No time signal at all — for universal truths: "The sun rises in the east."
Sentences Built from Time Signals
  • Every day → Arjun goes to school every day.
  • Always → The Dal Lake looks beautiful in winter.
  • On Sundays → Meera visits her grandmother on Sundays.
  • Universal truth (no signal) → Water boils at 100°C.
  • Generally → Kashmiri winters are very cold.
The Photograph Test

Simple Present describes your normal life — not a photograph of this moment. "I eat rice" = my habit. "I am eating rice" = snap a photo right now. If the signal says every / always / usually, choose Simple Present.

⚑ Common Mistake
  • ❌ "He go to school every day." → He goes (add -s/-es for he/she/it)
  • ❌ "I am eating breakfast every morning." → I eat breakfast every morning. (habit, not continuous)
  • ❌ Using "do" in questions for he/she: "Does she goes?" → Does she go? (no -s after does)
🔵
Present Continuous
Right now · At this moment · Temporary actions · Changing situations
▾

The Present Continuous is used when an action is happening at this exact moment, or is temporary — it is in progress during this period but will not last forever.

Formula
Subject + am / is / are + V-ing + Object
Negative: am/is/are + not + V-ing  |  Question: Am/Is/Are + Subject + V-ing?
Time Signals → Present Continuous
  • now right now at this moment — the strongest signals
  • at present currently at the moment
  • today this morning this evening — temporary ongoing action
  • this week these days — temporary situation over a short period
  • Look! Listen! — drawing attention to something happening right now
Sentences Built from Time Signals
  • Right now → Zara is reading a book right now.
  • At this moment → The snow is falling on Gulmarg at this moment.
  • This week → Rohan is preparing for his exams this week.
  • These days → More tourists are visiting Kashmir these days.
  • Look! → Look! The children are playing in the snow.
Big Action — Short Time Period

Think of it this way: the Present Continuous captures a large, ongoing action inside a short, specific time window. The action (studying, snowing, playing) is the "big thing" happening; "right now / this morning / this week" is the small time frame around it. This is the opposite of Simple Present, which describes a pattern with no specific moment.

⚑ Verbs That Cannot Be Continuous
  • State verbs are NEVER continuous: know, believe, understand, like, love, hate, want, need, seem, appear, own, belong, prefer
  • ❌ "I am knowing the answer." → I know the answer. ✓
  • ❌ "She is liking the food." → She likes the food. ✓
🟤
Present Perfect
Past action — present result · Life experience · Unfinished time periods
▾

The Present Perfect connects the past to the present. Something happened in the past, but we feel its result or importance NOW. Crucially, we often do NOT say exactly when — the exact time is unknown, unimportant, or we are using an unfinished time period.

Formula
Subject + have / has + V3 (Past Participle) + Object
have = I/you/we/they  |  has = he/she/it/singular name
Time Signals → Present Perfect
  • just — very recently finished: "She has just left."
  • already — sooner than expected: "I have already eaten."
  • yet — in negatives & questions: "Have you finished yet?"
  • ever never — life experience: "Have you ever seen the Dal Lake?"
  • so far until now up to now — up to this point in time
  • recently lately — in the recent past
  • this week / month / year — the period is NOT over yet
  • for + duration since + point in time — how long something has lasted
Sentences Built from Time Signals
  • Just → The bus has just arrived at the Srinagar station.
  • Already → Priya has already submitted her homework.
  • Ever → Have you ever visited the Mughal Gardens?
  • So far → We have read five chapters so far.
  • This year → The cricket team has won three matches this year.
  • Since 2020 → Aisha has lived in Srinagar since 2020.
The "No Exact Time" Rule

Present Perfect is used when you do NOT mention a finished, specific past time. The moment you say "yesterday / last year / in 2020 (as finished time)", you must switch to Simple Past.

✓ "I have seen that film." (no specific time)
✓ "I saw that film yesterday." (specific finished time = Simple Past)

⏳
Present Perfect Continuous
Started in the past — still continuing now · Emphasis on duration
▾

This tense emphasises how long an action has been going on — it started in the past and is still happening or has just stopped (but the effect is visible).

Formula
Subject + have / has + been + V-ing
Time Signals → Present Perfect Continuous
  • for + duration — "She has been studying for two hours."
  • since + starting point — "It has been raining since morning."
  • all day / all week / all morning
  • Visible result + duration together — "You look tired. Have you been running?"
Sentences Built from Time Signals
  • For two hours → Meera has been waiting for two hours.
  • Since morning → It has been snowing in Gulmarg since morning.
  • All day → The workers have been building the road all day.
Section 2 — Past Tenses & Their Time Signals
🔵
Simple Past
Big time period — small, completed action inside it
▾

The Simple Past describes an action that started and finished at a definite time in the past. The key is that there is a large, clearly named time period (yesterday, last week, in 1947), and the action is a single completed event inside it.

Formula
Subject + V2 (Past form) + Object
Negative: did + not + V1  |  Question: Did + Subject + V1?
Big Time — Small Action (The Container Image)

Imagine the time signal (yesterday / last year) as a large container. The action (went, ate, wrote) is a small object placed neatly inside that container. The action is complete — it does not spill out of the container into the present.

Time Signals → Simple Past
  • yesterday last night — "She left yesterday."
  • last week / month / year — "We won last year."
  • ago — "He arrived two days ago."
  • in + finished year — "India became independent in 1947."
  • on + specific date/day — "The match was on Monday."
  • when (for a sudden single event) — "The bell rang when I was sleeping." (the ringing = simple past)
  • once upon a time — stories and narratives
Sentences Built from Time Signals
  • Yesterday → Arjun played cricket yesterday.
  • Last week → Our school team won the inter-school competition last week.
  • Two years ago → My family visited Pahalgam two years ago.
  • In 1947 → India became independent in 1947.
  • On Sunday → Zara baked a cake on Sunday.
⚑ Common Mistake — Regular vs Irregular Verbs
  • Regular: add -ed → walk→walked, play→played, visit→visited
  • Irregular: must memorise → go→went, eat→ate, write→wrote, see→saw, buy→bought
  • ❌ "She goed to school." → She went to school. ✓
  • ❌ "I didn't went there." → I didn't go there. ✓ (V1 after did)
🌊
Past Continuous
Big ongoing action — small time period or interrupting event
▾

The Past Continuous describes an action that was in progress at a specific moment or period in the past. It is the exact opposite of Simple Past in structure: here the action is large and ongoing, while the time period is small or specific.

Formula
Subject + was / were + V-ing + Object
was = I/he/she/it  |  were = you/we/they
Big Action — Small Time (The River Image)

Imagine the action as a wide, flowing river (studying, sleeping, cooking). The time signal (at 7 pm / when the bell rang) is a small bridge over that river. You are standing on the bridge looking at the water flowing beneath — the action was happening all around that moment.

Time Signals → Past Continuous
  • at + time in the past — "She was cooking at 6 pm." (big action at a precise clock time)
  • while — "I was reading while he was sleeping." (two ongoing actions together)
  • when (for the background action) — "I was sleeping when the phone rang." (sleeping = continuous background)
  • all morning / all day / all night — ongoing through the time period
  • this time yesterday / last Sunday — point in past when action was ongoing
Sentences Built from Time Signals
  • At 8 pm → Meera was doing her homework at 8 pm.
  • While → Rohan was singing while Priya was playing the guitar.
  • When (background) → I was walking near Dal Lake when it started to rain.
  • All morning → The students were practising for the play all morning.
  • This time yesterday → We were travelling to Sonamarg this time yesterday.
When + Past Continuous + Simple Past Together

When two past events appear together with "when," the longer background action = Past Continuous, and the sudden shorter action = Simple Past.

"She was cooking (long) when the power went (sudden) off."
"The teacher was explaining when Arjun knocked on the door."

⏮️
Past Perfect
The "earlier past" — action completed before another past action
▾

The Past Perfect is used when you have two events in the past and you need to show which one happened first. The earlier action takes Past Perfect; the later action takes Simple Past.

Formula
Subject + had + V3 + Object
had = all subjects (I/you/he/she/it/we/they)
Time Signals → Past Perfect
  • before — "She had left before he arrived." (leaving came first)
  • after — "After he had eaten, he went to sleep." (eating came first)
  • by the time — "By the time we reached, the train had left."
  • already … when — "The film had already started when we entered."
  • as soon as … had — "As soon as she had finished, she called me."
  • In Reported Speech (indirect speech) — Simple Past in direct becomes Past Perfect in reported
Sentences Built from Time Signals
  • Before → Arjun had finished his project before the teacher arrived.
  • After → After the snow had melted, the roads reopened.
  • By the time → By the time we reached Gulmarg, the chairlift had closed.
  • Already … when → She had already eaten when we invited her.
Section 3 — Future Tenses & Their Time Signals
🟢
Simple Future
Predictions · Decisions made now · Offers · Promises
▾

The Simple Future is used for actions that will happen after the present moment — predictions, spontaneous decisions, offers, promises, and things we believe will happen.

Formula
Subject + will + V1 + Object
Negative: will not (won't) + V1  |  Question: Will + Subject + V1?
Time Signals → Simple Future
  • tomorrow the day after tomorrow
  • next week / month / year next Sunday
  • soon shortly in a moment
  • in + future time — "in ten minutes / in 2030"
  • probably certainly perhaps — predictions with will
  • No time signal — spontaneous decision: "The phone is ringing. I'll answer it."
Sentences Built from Time Signals
  • Tomorrow → It will snow in Gulmarg tomorrow.
  • Next year → Priya will study in Jammu next year.
  • In two hours → The train will arrive in two hours.
  • Probably (prediction) → They will probably win the match.
  • Spontaneous offer → Don't worry — I will help you carry those bags.
will vs going to

will = spontaneous decision or pure prediction ("Look at those clouds — it will rain.")
going to = planned decision made before the moment of speaking ("I am going to study tonight." — already planned)
Both are correct future forms; context and intention decide which to use.

🌀
Future Continuous
Big ongoing action — at a specific future time point
▾

The Future Continuous describes an action that will be in progress at a specific moment in the future. The action is the "big, ongoing thing" and the time signal is the small, precise point.

Formula
Subject + will be + V-ing
Time Signals → Future Continuous
  • at + future time — "At 8 pm tonight, I will be watching the match."
  • this time tomorrow — "This time tomorrow, we will be travelling to Leh."
  • all day / all evening tomorrow
  • when you arrive / call — "When you arrive, I will be sleeping."
Sentences Built from Time Signals
  • At 9 am tomorrow → The students will be writing their exam at 9 am tomorrow.
  • This time tomorrow → We will be flying over the Himalayas this time tomorrow.
  • When you call → When you call, she will be cooking dinner.
✅
Future Perfect
Action completed before a specific future deadline
▾

The Future Perfect says that an action will be finished before a certain point in the future. The time signal always mentions a future deadline.

Formula
Subject + will have + V3
Time Signals → Future Perfect
  • by + future time — "By 6 pm, I will have finished."
  • by the time + future event — "By the time you arrive, we will have cooked dinner."
  • before + future event — "Before the match ends, they will have scored."
  • by next week / month / year
Sentences Built from Time Signals
  • By tonight → Aisha will have completed her project by tonight.
  • By the time you return → By the time you return from Sonamarg, we will have prepared dinner.
  • By next year → The new bridge over the Jhelum will have been completed by next year.
Section 4 — The Master Signal Chart: Signal → Tense
🗝️
How to Read a Sentence and Pick the Right Tense
A step-by-step method for any sentence
▾
1
Find the time signal first
Underline the adverb, phrase, or clause: yesterday / now / by tomorrow / for two hours / since Monday. This is your key.
2
Identify the time zone: Past / Present / Future
Words like yesterday, last, ago, in 1947 = Past zone. Words like now, today, this week, still = Present zone. Words like tomorrow, next, soon, by + future = Future zone.
3
Ask: Is the action a habit, a moment, or a duration?
Habit / general truth → Simple.   Ongoing at a moment → Continuous.   Completed with present/future result or deadline → Perfect.   Duration emphasised → Perfect Continuous.
4
Combine: Zone + Type = Tense
Present + Habit = Simple Present. Past + Ongoing = Past Continuous. Future + Completed before deadline = Future Perfect. Present + Duration = Present Perfect Continuous.
5
Apply the correct formula
Use the right auxiliary + verb form, match subject (he/she → is, has, was) and write the sentence confidently.
Practise This Method — Example

Sentence: "She _____ (study) for three hours."
Step 1: Signal = for three hours (duration). Step 2: No other signal → present zone. Step 3: Duration + still going. Step 4: Present + Duration = Present Perfect Continuous. Step 5: She has been studying for three hours. ✓

  • always, usually, often, sometimes, rarely, never
  • every day / week / month / year
  • on Sundays / Mondays… — day-based routines
  • in the morning / afternoon / evening
  • generally, normally, regularly
  • no time signal — universal truths (Water boils at 100°C)
  • Formula: S + V1(s/es)

  • now, right now, at this moment
  • at present, currently, at the moment
  • today, this morning / evening
  • this week, these days
  • Look! Listen! — drawing attention
  • Formula: S + am/is/are + V-ing
🟤 Present Perfect — Signal Words
  • just, already, yet, ever, never
  • so far, until now, up to now
  • recently, lately
  • this week / month / year — period not yet over
  • for + duration, since + starting point
  • Formula: S + have/has + V3
⏳ Present Perfect Continuous — Signal Words
  • for + duration — for two hours, for months
  • since + point in time — since morning, since 2020
  • all day / all morning / all week
  • Formula: S + have/has been + V-ing
🔵 Simple Past — Signal Words
  • yesterday, last night
  • last week / month / year
  • ago — two days ago, years ago
  • in + finished year — in 1947, in 2010
  • on + specific date — on Monday, on 15th August
  • when — sudden completed action
  • Formula: S + V2
🌊 Past Continuous — Signal Words
  • at + past time — at 7 pm, at midnight
  • while — two ongoing past actions
  • when — the longer background action
  • all day / all morning / all night
  • this time yesterday / last Sunday
  • Formula: S + was/were + V-ing
⏮️ Past Perfect — Signal Words
  • before — earlier of two past actions
  • after — the action that came first
  • by the time — "By the time he arrived…"
  • already … when — "had already left when…"
  • as soon as … had
  • Formula: S + had + V3
🟢 Simple Future — Signal Words
  • tomorrow, the day after tomorrow
  • next week / month / year / Sunday
  • soon, shortly, in a moment
  • in + future period — in ten minutes, in 2030
  • probably, certainly, perhaps
  • no signal — spontaneous decisions
  • Formula: S + will + V1
🌀 Future Continuous — Signal Words
  • at + future time — at 8 pm tonight
  • this time tomorrow / next week
  • all day / all evening tomorrow
  • when you arrive / call
  • Formula: S + will be + V-ing
✅ Future Perfect — Signal Words
  • by + future time — by 6 pm, by Monday
  • by the time + future clause
  • before + future event
  • by next week / year
  • Formula: S + will have + V3
⚑ Critical Rules — Never Forget
  • yesterday / ago / last / in (finished year) → always Simple Past, NEVER Present Perfect
  • State verbs (know, like, want, own…) → NEVER use with continuous tenses
  • He/She/It + Simple Present → always add -s/-es to verb
  • did + not → always V1 (not V2): didn't go ✓, didn't went ✗
  • for = duration (for 3 hours) → Perfect / Perfect Continuous
  • since = starting point (since Monday) → Perfect / Perfect Continuous
  • while = two actions together → both Continuous (same tense)
  • when + short action → Simple Past; when + background → Past Continuous
