These are the technical names used in English grammar — the vocabulary of grammar itself. Knowing these terms allows you to understand question instructions, recognise what is being tested, and read grammar explanations confidently. They appear in instructions, metalinguistic questions, and comprehension passages in CDS/NDA/AFCAT papers.

Verb Forms & Non-Finite Structures
Infinitive
Also: To-Infinitive · Bare Infinitive
The base form of a verb. The to-infinitive includes "to" before the base form; the bare infinitive omits "to" (used after modals and certain verbs).
To-infinitive: "He decided to advance."  |  Bare: "He must report now."
Gerund
Also: Verbal Noun · -ing Noun
A verb ending in -ing that functions as a noun. It can be a subject, object, or complement — but it is NOT an adjective or part of a verb phrase.
"Marching builds discipline." (subject)  |  "He enjoys commanding." (object)
Participle
Present Participle · Past Participle · Participial Phrase
A verb form used as an adjective or as part of a verb tense. The present participle ends in -ing; the past participle is V3 (the third principal part).
Present: "the advancing troops"  |  Past: "the defeated enemy"  |  In tense: "She has completed training."
Participle Phrase
Also: Participial Clause · Dangling Participle (error form)
A phrase beginning with a participle that modifies a noun. It must be placed immediately next to the noun it modifies — if separated, it becomes a dangling participle (a common exam error).
✓ "Exhausted by the march, the soldiers rested." (modifies soldiers)
✗ "Exhausted by the march, the tent was set up." (dangles — tent can't be exhausted)
Infinitive Phrase
Also: Infinitive Clause
A phrase starting with a to-infinitive that acts as a noun, adjective, or adverb within the sentence.
"To lead from the front is the officer's duty." (noun — subject)  |  "He trained to qualify." (adverb — purpose)
Finite Verb
Also: Tensed Verb · Predicate Verb
A verb that is marked for tense, person, and number. Every complete sentence must contain at least one finite verb — it is the main predicate of the clause.
"The officer commands the platoon." — 'commands' is finite (present, 3rd person singular)
Non-Finite Verb
Infinitive · Gerund · Participle
A verb form that is not marked for tense or person — it cannot alone serve as the predicate of a sentence. The three non-finite forms are the infinitive, gerund, and participle.
"To win requires sacrifice." — 'to win' = non-finite (no tense/person marker)
Transitive Verb
Also: Vt · Takes a Direct Object
A verb that requires a direct object to complete its meaning. The action passes from the subject through the verb to the object.
"She leads the unit." — 'leads' is transitive; 'the unit' is the direct object.
Intransitive Verb
Also: Vi · No Direct Object
A verb that does not require a direct object — its meaning is complete without one. Intransitive verbs cannot be made passive.
"The troops marched." / "The sun rose." / "He arrived." — no object possible
Ditransitive Verb
Also: Double-Object Verb
A verb that takes two objects: a direct object (what) and an indirect object (to whom / for whom).
"He gave the platoon [IO] its orders [DO]."  |  "She taught the recruits [IO] fieldcraft [DO]."
Linking Verb
Also: Copula · Copular Verb · Be-Verb
A verb that connects the subject to a subject complement (predicate adjective or predicate noun) rather than expressing action. The subject complement describes or renames the subject.
be, seem, appear, become, feel, look, smell, taste, sound, remain, grow, turn
"The officer seems confident." ('confident' = predicate adjective describing the officer)
Auxiliary Verb
Also: Helping Verb · Primary Auxiliary
A verb that accompanies a main verb to express tense, voice, mood, or aspect. The three primary auxiliaries are be, have, and do.
"He is training." / "She has completed." / "Do you agree?"
Modal Auxiliary
Also: Modal Verb · Defective Verb
An auxiliary verb that expresses possibility, ability, permission, obligation, or condition. Modals are "defective" — they have no infinitive, no -ing form, no -s in 3rd person, and no past participle.
can, could, may, might, shall, should, will, would, must, ought to, used to, need, dare
Semi-Modal
Also: Marginal Modal · Quasi-Modal
Verbs that behave sometimes like modals, sometimes like ordinary verbs — they have two patterns of use depending on context.
need, dare, used to, ought to, have to, be able to, be going to
"He need not report." (modal) vs "He needs to report." (main verb)
Stative Verb
Also: State Verb · Non-Action Verb
A verb that describes a state or condition rather than an action. Stative verbs do not normally appear in progressive (-ing) tenses.
know, believe, understand, want, own, contain, seem, appear, resemble
✗ "I am knowing." → ✓ "I know."
Dynamic Verb
Also: Action Verb · Event Verb
A verb that describes a deliberate action or process — the opposite of a stative verb. Dynamic verbs can be used in progressive tenses.
run, fight, build, write, march, command, train, eat, speak, decide
Phrasal Verb
Also: Multi-Word Verb · Verb-Particle Combination
A verb combined with a preposition or adverb particle to create a new meaning that differs from the individual words.
"call off" (cancel) / "give up" (surrender) / "look into" (investigate) / "carry out" (execute) / "stand down" (withdraw)
Verb Moods
Indicative Mood
Most common mood — statements & questions
Used to state facts, ask questions, or make ordinary assertions. This is the default mood of the verb in most sentences.
"The army advances at dawn." / "Did the officer report?" / "She completed the course."
Imperative Mood
Also: Command Form · Directive Mood
Used to give commands, instructions, requests, or invitations. The subject (you) is understood and not stated. The verb is in base form.
"Report to HQ immediately." / "Stand at ease." / "Do not enter the restricted zone."
Subjunctive Mood
Also: Conditional / Hypothetical / Optative (in wishes)
Used for hypothetical situations, wishes, conditions contrary to fact, and formal demands/suggestions. In modern English it survives mainly in: (1) "were" for all persons in unreal conditions; (2) bare base form after that-clauses of demand/suggestion.
Hypothetical: "If I were the CO…" (not 'was')  |  Demand: "The board recommends that he be present." (not 'is')
Conditional Mood
Also: Part of Subjunctive / Would-clause
Expresses an action or state dependent on a condition. Formed with would/could/might + base verb (in the main clause of conditionals).
"If he trained harder, he would pass." / "She could have been selected."
Interrogative Mood
Also: Question Form (not a classical mood in all grammar traditions)
The form of a sentence used to ask a question. Formed by inverting the subject and auxiliary verb, or by using a question word (who, what, where, when, why, how).
"Has he reported?" (inversion)  |  "Where is the convoy?" (wh-question)
Tense, Aspect & Voice
Aspect
Simple · Progressive (Continuous) · Perfect · Perfect Progressive
Aspect describes how an action unfolds in time — whether it is ongoing, completed, habitual, or was completed before another event. It combines with tense (past/present/future).
Simple: "He marched."  |  Progressive: "He was marching."  |  Perfect: "He had marched."  |  Perfect Progressive: "He had been marching."
Active Voice
vs Passive Voice
In the active voice, the grammatical subject performs the action. The sentence structure is Subject → Verb → Object.
"The officer signed the orders." (subject = doer)
Passive Voice
Structure: be + past participle (+ by agent)
In the passive voice, the grammatical subject receives the action. Formed with be + V3. The original doer may appear as "by + agent" or be omitted.
"The orders were signed by the officer." (subject = receiver)  |  "The base was secured." (agent omitted)
Sequence of Tenses
Also: Tense Harmony · Backshift (in reported speech)
The grammatical rule requiring that when the main clause is in a past tense, the subordinate clause verb must also shift to a past form. Exceptions apply to universal truths and current facts.
"He said he was ready." (backshift: is → was)  |  Exception: "He said water boils at 100°C." (universal truth stays present)

Independent vs Dependent Clauses
Main Clause · Subordinate Clause · Key structural distinction
▾
TermDefinitionExample
Independent Clause
(Main Clause)		A clause that expresses a complete thought and can stand alone as a sentence. It has a subject and a finite verb."The convoy arrived at dawn." — complete by itself ✓
Dependent Clause
(Subordinate Clause)		A clause that cannot stand alone — it depends on a main clause to complete its meaning. Introduced by a subordinating conjunction or relative pronoun."Although the road was clear, the convoy was delayed." — cannot stand alone ✓
Co-ordinate ClauseTwo or more independent clauses of equal grammatical rank joined by a coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS)."The officer gave the order and the troops advanced."

Adverb Clause Sub-Types — All Tested
  • Time: when, while, as, before, after, since, until, as soon as, by the time
  • Cause / Reason: because, since, as, for, inasmuch as
  • Condition: if, unless, provided that, as long as, in case, supposing
  • Concession / Contrast: although, though, even though, even if, whereas, while
  • Purpose: so that, in order that, lest (negative purpose)
  • Result / Consequence: so…that, such…that
  • Manner: as if, as though, as (in the same way that)
  • Comparison: than, as…as, the more…the more
  • Place: where, wherever

Defining vs Non-Defining Relative Clauses
Restrictive vs Non-Restrictive · Comma usage · "That" vs "Which"
▾
TypeDefinitionPunctuationPronoun
Defining (Restrictive)Identifies which specific noun is meant — essential to the meaning; its removal changes the sentence meaningNo commaswho / whom / that / which
Non-Defining (Non-Restrictive)Adds extra information about an already-identified noun — not essential; can be removed without changing the core meaningSet off by commaswho / whom / which (NOT "that")
Contrast Examples
  • Defining: "The officer who led the patrol was decorated." (identifies which officer — no commas) ✓
  • Non-defining: "Captain Roy, who led the patrol, was decorated." (Roy is already identified — commas required) ✓
  • ✗ "Captain Roy, that led the patrol, was decorated." → non-defining clauses cannot use "that"

Other Key Grammar Terms
Antecedent · Apposition · Ellipsis · Concord · Inversion · Dangling Modifier
▾
TermDefinitionExample / Note
AntecedentThe noun or noun phrase that a pronoun refers back to. Pronoun and antecedent must agree in number, gender, and person."The officer submitted his report." — 'officer' is the antecedent of 'his'
Apposition / AppositiveA noun or noun phrase placed next to another noun to rename or explain it. Usually set off by commas."Captain Roy, the commanding officer, gave the briefing."
EllipsisThe omission of words understood from context. Common in comparisons and after auxiliaries."She trains harder than he [does]." / "He can march and [he can] swim."
ConcordAgreement between grammatically related words — most commonly subject-verb agreement and pronoun-antecedent agreement."Each of the officers has his duty." (concord of number)
InversionReversing the normal subject-verb order. Occurs in questions, after negative adverbs fronted for emphasis, and in conditionals without "if"."Never have I seen such courage." / "Had he known, he would have acted." (conditional inversion)
Dangling ModifierA modifier (usually a participial phrase) that does not logically attach to the intended word — a common exam error.✗ "Running through the forest, the bridge appeared." → Bridge can't run. ✓ "Running through the forest, he saw the bridge."
Misplaced ModifierA modifier placed too far from the word it modifies, creating ambiguity or wrong meaning.✗ "He almost drove his car to Delhi." → ✓ "He drove his car almost to Delhi."
Subject ComplementA noun, pronoun, or adjective that follows a linking verb and describes or renames the subject."She is an officer." (predicate noun) / "He seems confident." (predicate adjective)
Object ComplementA noun or adjective that follows a direct object and describes or renames it."They elected him captain." / "She found the task difficult."
ParallelismUsing the same grammatical form for elements that are equal in thought — essential for lists, comparisons, and correlative conjunctions.✗ "He likes to march, swimming, and rest." → ✓ "He likes marching, swimming, and resting."
Reported SpeechAlso called Indirect Speech — reporting what someone said without quoting directly. Requires tense backshift, pronoun changes, and time/place adverb changes.Direct: "I will report tomorrow." → Indirect: "He said he would report the next day."
Cleft SentenceA construction that divides a simple sentence into two clauses for emphasis — using "it is/was…that/who" or "what…is/was"."It was the general who made the decision." / "What surprised us was his calm."
Quick Reference — Term Groups at a Glance
Verb Non-Finite Forms
  • Infinitive: to + base ("to march") or bare ("must march")
  • Gerund: -ing form acting as noun ("Training is essential")
  • Present Participle: -ing form as adjective ("the marching troops")
  • Past Participle: V3 form as adjective or in perfect/passive ("completed")
  • Participial Phrase: phrase starting with participle — must touch its noun
Five Verb Moods
  • Indicative: facts & statements ("He marched.")
  • Imperative: commands ("March!")
  • Subjunctive: hypothetical / wish ("If I were…" / "that he be present")
  • Conditional: result of condition ("would pass")
  • Interrogative: questions ("Did he report?")
Pronoun Types — All 13
  • Personal: I, you, he, she, it, we, they
  • Possessive: mine, yours, his, hers (stand alone)
  • Reflexive: myself, himself (action back to subject)
  • Emphatic: same form — adds stress, removable
  • Demonstrative: this, that, these, those
  • Interrogative: who, whom, what, which, whose
  • Relative: who, whom, which, that (in relative clause)
  • Indefinite: everyone, someone, none, each, both
  • Reciprocal: each other, one another
  • Impersonal: it / one (dummy or generic)
  • Distributive: each, either, neither (one at a time)
  • Dummy / Expletive: there / it (structural fillers)
Clause Types
  • Main / Independent: stands alone; complete thought
  • Subordinate / Dependent: cannot stand alone
  • Noun Clause: acts as noun (that, what, whether…)
  • Adjective / Relative Clause: modifies noun (who, which, that…)
  • Adverb Clause: modifies verb — time, cause, condition, contrast, purpose, result, manner
  • Defining: no commas; essential; uses "that"
  • Non-Defining: commas; extra info; no "that"
Sentence Structure Types
  • Simple: one independent clause
  • Compound: 2+ independent clauses (FANBOYS / semicolon)
  • Complex: 1 independent + 1+ dependent clause
  • Compound-Complex: 2+ independent + 1+ dependent
Modifiers — Errors to Know
  • Dangling Modifier: modifier has no logical noun to attach to in the sentence
  • Misplaced Modifier: modifier is too far from the word it modifies
  • Squinting Modifier: ambiguously placed — could modify words on either side
  • Rule: modifier must be placed as close as possible to the word it modifies
Conjunction Types
  • Coordinating: FANBOYS — join equal elements
  • Subordinating: introduce dependent clauses — because, although, if, when, since, unless
  • Correlative: pairs — both…and, either…or, neither…nor, not only…but also
  • Conjunctive Adverb: however, therefore, moreover (connect independent clauses)
Key Structural Terms
  • Antecedent: noun a pronoun refers back to
  • Appositive: noun renaming another noun (commas)
  • Ellipsis: omitted words understood from context
  • Concord: grammatical agreement (S-V, pronoun-antecedent)
  • Inversion: reversed subject-auxiliary order
  • Parallelism: equal structures for equal ideas
  • Cleft Sentence: "It was X that…" — for emphasis
