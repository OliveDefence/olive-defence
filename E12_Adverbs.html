E12 — Adverbs
Types, degrees, position rules, common confusables & exam-pattern traps for CDS · NDA · AFCAT
CDS NDA AFCAT 5 Sections 15 Questions

An adverb modifies a verb, adjective, another adverb, or an entire clause. It answers questions like How? When? Where? How often? To what degree? Adverbs are tested in CDS/NDA/AFCAT primarily through error spotting, sentence improvement, and fill-in-the-blank question types. The key trap areas are: position of adverbs, adjective vs adverb confusion, double negatives, and irregular degree forms.

Section 1 — Types of Adverbs
🗂️
Classification of Adverbs
Manner · Place · Time · Frequency · Degree · Sentence adverbs
▾

Adverbs are grouped by the question they answer:

TypeQuestion AnsweredExamples
MannerHow?bravely, quickly, well, hard, fast
PlaceWhere?here, there, abroad, nearby, everywhere
TimeWhen?now, then, soon, already, yet, still
FrequencyHow often?always, usually, often, seldom, never, once
DegreeTo what extent?very, quite, rather, almost, too, enough, fairly
SentenceComments on the whole clauseunfortunately, clearly, obviously, frankly
InterrogativeAsks a questionwhere, when, why, how
RelativeIntroduces a relative clausewhere, when, why (after noun)
Examples in context
  • The cadet marched briskly. [Manner — How?]
  • The convoy stopped here. [Place — Where?]
  • The orders arrived soon. [Time — When?]
  • He always reports on time. [Frequency — How often?]
  • The mission was quite successful. [Degree — To what extent?]
  • Fortunately, no casualties were reported. [Sentence adverb]
🔤
Formation of Adverbs
Adding -ly, spelling changes, and flat adverbs (fast, hard, late)
▾

Most adverbs of manner are formed by adding -ly to an adjective, but spelling rules and exceptions must be noted:

Formation Rules
  • Most adjectives: add -ly → brave → bravely; quick → quickly
  • Adjective ending in -y: change y→i, add -ly → happy → happily; easy → easily
  • Adjective ending in -le: drop e, add -y → simple → simply; gentle → gently
  • Adjective ending in -ic: add -ally → basic → basically; tragic → tragically (exception: publicly)
  • Adjective ending in -ful: add -ly → careful → carefully; hopeful → hopefully
  • Adjective ending in -ll: add -y → full → fully; dull → dully
⚑ Flat Adverbs — Same form as the adjective
  • fast — He drives fast. (not "fastly") ✓
  • hard — She worked hard. (not "hardly" — "hardly" means scarcely)
  • late — He arrived late. (not "lately" — "lately" means recently)
  • high — The plane flew high. (not "highly" — "highly" = to a great degree: "highly trained")
  • near — Come near. (not "nearly" — "nearly" = almost)
  • deep — They dug deep. (not "deeply" — "deeply" = intensely/emotionally)
Section 2 — Position of Adverbs
📍
Position Rules — Manner, Place, Time
End position default · MPT order · Never split infinitive
▾

When multiple adverbs follow a verb, they follow the order: Manner → Place → Time (MPT). This is tested in sentence rearrangement questions.

1
Manner
2
Place
3
Time
MPT Order Examples
  • The soldiers marched bravely [M] through the valley [P] at dawn [T]. ✓
  • She performed brilliantly [M] on stage [P] last night [T]. ✓
  • He drove carefully [M] to the base [P] yesterday [T]. ✓
Wrong Order Errors
  • ✗ "He arrived yesterday at the base quickly." → ✓ "He arrived quickly at the base yesterday."
  • ✗ "She reported at 0600 to headquarters promptly." → ✓ "…promptly at headquarters at 0600."
Split Infinitive — Avoid in Formal Writing

Placing an adverb between "to" and the verb is called a split infinitive. While acceptable in modern informal usage, it is treated as an error in defence exams.

✗ "The officer decided to quickly retreat." → ✓ "…decided to retreat quickly."
✗ "We need to carefully examine the report." → ✓ "…need to examine the report carefully."

🔁
Position of Frequency Adverbs
always, usually, often, seldom, never — before main verb, after auxiliary
▾

Frequency adverbs (always, usually, often, sometimes, seldom, rarely, never) have a fixed position in the sentence — this is a heavily tested rule in error-spotting questions.

Position Rule for Frequency Adverbs
  • Before the main verb: She always reports on time. ✓
  • After the auxiliary verb (be, have, will, can, must…): He is always punctual. / She has never failed. ✓
  • After "be" when used alone: They are usually ready. ✓
  • At beginning or end (for emphasis only — especially sometimes/usually/often): Often, the orders change at the last moment. ✓
Common Position Errors
  • ✗ "He reports always on time." → ✓ "He always reports on time."
  • ✗ "She has completed never a task late." → ✓ "She has never completed a task late."
  • ✗ "The unit is seldom late — seldom it misses briefing." → ✓ "The unit is seldom late; it seldom misses briefing."
⚑ "Seldom / Rarely / Never" — Inversion After Fronting
  • When seldom, rarely, or never is placed at the start of a sentence for emphasis, the subject and auxiliary are inverted.
  • Seldom does the commanding officer repeat an order. ✓
  • Never have I seen such discipline. ✓
  • ✗ "Seldom the CO repeats an order." ← No inversion = error
Section 3 — Degrees of Comparison
📊
Forming Degrees of Adverbs
Regular (-er/-est vs more/most) · Irregular forms · Flat adverbs
▾

Like adjectives, adverbs have three degrees: Positive, Comparative, Superlative. One-syllable flat adverbs take -er/-est; adverbs ending in -ly take more/most.

Degree Formation Rules
  • One-syllable flat adverb: fast → faster → fastest; hard → harder → hardest
  • -ly adverbs: quickly → more quickly → most quickly; bravely → more bravely → most bravely
  • Irregular: well → better → best; badly → worse → worst; little → less → least; much → more → most
Double Comparison Errors (very common trap)
  • ✗ "She ran more faster than him." → ✓ "She ran faster than him."
  • ✗ "He performed more better today." → ✓ "He performed better today."
  • ✗ "They arrived most earliest." → ✓ "They arrived earliest."
Section 4 — Adjective vs Adverb Confusion
⚖️
Adjective vs Adverb — The Core Distinction
Linking verbs take adjectives · Action verbs take adverbs · "Good" vs "well"
▾

The most heavily tested error type is using an adjective where an adverb is needed, or vice versa. The key rule: linking verbs (be, seem, look, feel, smell, taste, sound, become, appear, remain) are followed by adjectives (describing the subject), while action verbs are followed by adverbs (describing the action).

Linking Verb → Adjective
  • The cadet looked confident. (not "confidently" — "looked" = linking verb here) ✓
  • The stew smells good. (not "well") ✓
  • He remained calm under fire. ✓
  • She felt nervous before the interview. ✓
Action Verb → Adverb
  • The cadet performed confidently. (action verb — describe the action) ✓
  • She answered the questions well. (not "good") ✓
  • He fought bravely throughout the engagement. ✓
⚑ "Good" vs "Well" — The Defining Exam Trap
  • Good = adjective only → "She is a good officer." / "He feels good." (= healthy)
  • Well = adverb (manner) → "She performed well." / also adjective meaning "in good health" → "He is well now."
  • ✗ "She performed good in the drill." → ✓ "She performed well in the drill."
  • ✗ "He is doing good in training." → ✓ "He is doing well in training."

Section 5 — Common Exam Traps
🪤
Double Negatives & Negative Adverbs
hardly, scarcely, barely, seldom — already negative; never add "not"
▾

Hardly, scarcely, barely, seldom, rarely, never are negative adverbs. Using them with another negative word ("not", "no", "nothing") creates an illegal double negative in formal English.

Double Negative Errors
  • ✗ "He couldn't hardly breathe." → ✓ "He could hardly breathe."
  • ✗ "She didn't scarcely have time." → ✓ "She scarcely had time."
  • ✗ "I don't never arrive late." → ✓ "I never arrive late." / "I don't ever arrive late."
  • ✗ "There was nothing hardly left." → ✓ "There was hardly anything left."
Scarcely / Hardly — Inversion Rule

Like seldom and never, when scarcely or hardly begins a sentence for emphasis, the subject and auxiliary must be inverted.

Hardly had the troops assembled when the orders changed. ✓
Scarcely had she sat down when the alarm sounded. ✓

Note: "hardly…when" and "scarcely…when" (not "than") — this is a common error.

⚑ Hardly/Scarcely … when (NOT "than")
  • ✗ "Hardly had he entered than the general arrived." → ✓ "…entered when the general arrived."
  • ✗ "Scarcely had they landed than firing began." → ✓ "…landed when firing began."
  • (Compare: "No sooner had he entered than the general arrived." — "No sooner" takes "than" ✓)
💡
Too vs Very vs Enough
"Too" implies excess (negative); "enough" follows the adjective/adverb
▾

Too, very, and enough are degree adverbs with distinct rules — frequently tested in error spotting.

Key Rules
  • Too = excessive (implies a negative result): "The pack is too heavy to carry." ✓
  • Very = intensifier with no negative implication: "The pack is very heavy." ✓ — no result clause needed
  • Enough comes AFTER the adjective or adverb it modifies: "He is tall enough to qualify." ✓
  • Enough comes BEFORE a noun: "She has enough courage." ✓
Common Errors
  • ✗ "He is too brave." → (Implies he's excessively brave — a negative outcome must follow; ✓ "He is very brave." if no negative result)
  • ✗ "She is enough strong to lead." → ✓ "She is strong enough to lead." (enough comes after the adjective)
  • ✗ "The cadet is very short to reach the beam." → ✓ "The cadet is too short to reach the beam." (negative consequence → use "too")
🔀
Confusable Adverbs — Yet, Still, Already, Anymore
Positive vs negative contexts · Tense dependencies
▾
AdverbUseExample
yetNegative sentences & questions (perfect tense); expectation unfulfilled"Has the general arrived yet?" / "He hasn't reported yet." ✓
stillOngoing action (affirmative/negative); earlier than expected"He is still training." / "She has still not received orders." ✓
alreadyAction completed sooner than expected (affirmative)"He has already submitted the report." ✓
anymore / any longerCessation of an action (negative sentences)"She doesn't serve in that unit anymore." ✓
⚑ "Yet" vs "Already" in Questions
  • "Has he arrived yet?" = neutral question (not sure if he has) ✓
  • "Has he already arrived?" = surprised that he arrived sooner than expected ✓
  • ✗ "He has yet arrived." → ✓ "He has already arrived." / "He hasn't arrived yet."

Quick Reference — Core Rules
Adverb Position — MPT Order
  • Manner → Place → Time: "She drove carefully [M] to the base [P] at noon [T]." ✓
  • Frequency adverbs before main verb, after auxiliary
  • Degree adverbs (very, quite, rather, too) immediately before the word they modify
  • Enough comes after adjective/adverb, before noun
  • Avoid split infinitive in formal exam writing
Negative Adverbs — No Double Negatives
  • Hardly, scarcely, barely, seldom, rarely, never are already negative
  • ✗ "can't hardly" → ✓ "can hardly"
  • Hardly/Scarcely…when (inversion + "when", not "than")
  • No sooner…than (inversion + "than")
  • Seldom/Never/Rarely at sentence start → auxiliary inversion required
Adjective vs Adverb
  • Linking verbs (be, seem, look, feel, sound, smell, taste) → Adjective
  • Action verbs → Adverb
  • Good = adjective only; Well = adverb of manner (+ adj = healthy)
  • ✗ "performed good" → ✓ "performed well"
  • Flat adverbs: fast, hard, late, high, near, deep — do NOT add -ly
Too / Very / Enough
  • Too = excess with negative consequence → "too heavy to carry"
  • Very = intensifier, no consequence needed → "very heavy"
  • Enough after adj/adv: "strong enough to lead"
  • Enough before noun: "enough time"
  • ✗ "enough strong" → ✓ "strong enough"
Yet / Still / Already / Anymore
  • Yet — questions & negatives (perfect tense): "hasn't arrived yet"
  • Still — ongoing: "is still training"; before auxiliary in negative: "has still not"
  • Already — completed sooner than expected: "has already finished"
  • Anymore / any longer — cessation, negative sentences: "doesn't serve there anymore"
Irregular Degree — Must Know
  • well → better → best
  • badly → worse → worst
  • much → more → most
  • little → less → least
  • far → farther/further → farthest/furthest
  • ✗ "more better" / ✗ "more faster" — double comparison
