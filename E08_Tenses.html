English Grammar · Tenses

Tenses —
Time, Aspect & Sequence

Tense errors are the single largest source of marks lost in CDS, NDA and AFCAT English. Wrong aspect choice, broken sequence of tenses, stative verbs in continuous form, and conditional-clause mismatches — this module breaks down every high-frequency pattern.

Comprehensive CDS · NDA · AFCAT 20 Practice Questions

English has twelve tenses — four aspects (simple, continuous, perfect, perfect continuous) across three times (past, present, future). Most exam errors are not about memorising tense names; they are about four key areas: aspect choice (simple vs perfect vs continuous), stative vs dynamic verbs, sequence of tenses in complex sentences, and conditional structures. Master these and nearly all tense questions become straightforward.

Section 2 — The Most Tested Distinctions
⚡
Simple Past vs Present Perfect
The most common tense error in CDS/NDA spotting errors
▾

This is the single most tested tense distinction across all three exams. The rule: use Simple Past for completed actions with a definite past time; use Present Perfect for actions with no specified past time or with a present relevance.

The Core Distinction

Simple Past = finished, at a specific time → "He left yesterday."
Present Perfect = finished, time unspecified OR still relevant now → "He has left." / "He has served for ten years [and still is]."

Key trigger: Words like yesterday, ago, last week, in 2010, then → always Simple Past.
Words like just, already, yet, recently, ever, never, since, for → always Present Perfect.

Classic Errors
  • I have seen him yesterday. → I saw him yesterday. ✓ (definite past time)
  • She joined the academy recently. → She has joined the academy recently. ✓
  • He has left two years ago. → He left two years ago. ✓
⚑ "Since" and "For" with Present Perfect
  • "Since" marks a point of time in the past: since 2010, since Monday, since he joined
  • "For" marks a duration of time: for two years, for three months
  • Both always trigger Present Perfect (or Past Perfect in past context)
  • ✗ "He is serving for ten years." → ✓ "He has been serving for ten years."
🔄
Stative Verbs — Never Use Continuous Form
Know, believe, want, see, have… cannot be progressive
▾

Stative verbs describe states, not actions. They express mental states, emotions, senses, possession, or appearance — and cannot be used in any continuous (progressive) tense.

Common Stative Verbs — Never Use -ing Form
  • Mental states: know, believe, think (= opinion), understand, remember, forget, mean, suppose
  • Emotions: love, hate, like, prefer, want, wish, need, fear
  • Senses: see, hear, smell, taste, feel (when describing passive sense)
  • Possession/state: have (= possess), own, belong, contain, consist, include, resemble
  • Appearance: seem, appear, look (= seem), sound
Errors
  • I am knowing the answer. → I know the answer. ✓
  • He is having a car. → He has a car. ✓ (possession)
  • She is believing in discipline. → She believes in discipline. ✓
⚑ Dual-Use Verbs — Stative AND Dynamic
  • have: "He has a rifle." (possess = stative ✓) vs "He is having lunch." (activity = dynamic ✓)
  • think: "I think he is right." (opinion = stative ✓) vs "I am thinking about the plan." (process = dynamic ✓)
  • see: "I see the mountain." (passive sense = stative ✓) vs "I am seeing the doctor." (meeting = dynamic ✓)
  • taste / smell: "The food tastes good." (state) vs "She is tasting the soup." (active testing)
⏱️
Past Perfect — Sequence of Two Past Actions
The earlier action → Past Perfect; the later action → Simple Past
▾

When two actions both happened in the past, the earlier one must use Past Perfect (had + V3) and the later one uses Simple Past (V2). This shows the sequence clearly.

The Sequence Rule

Earlier action → had + V3  |  Later action → V2

"When the reinforcements arrived [later], the enemy had already retreated [earlier]." ✓
"He had briefed [earlier] the team before the operation began [later]." ✓

Errors — Both actions in Simple Past
  • By the time we reached, the train already left. → …the train had already left. ✓
  • He told me that he finished his report. → …he had finished his report. ✓
⚑ When Past Perfect is NOT needed

If the sequence is already made clear by "before" or "after", Past Perfect is technically optional — Simple Past can be used for both.
"He ate before he slept." ✓ (sequence obvious from "before")
But if there is no sequence connector, Past Perfect is mandatory to show which happened first.

Section 3 — Conditionals & Reported Speech
🔀
Conditional Sentences — All Four Types
Type 0, 1, 2 and 3 — the tense combinations are fixed
▾
TypeIf-clauseMain clauseUse
Type 0Simple PresentSimple PresentAlways-true facts / scientific truths
Type 1Simple Presentwill + V1Real future possibility
Type 2Simple Pastwould + V1Unreal / hypothetical present
Type 3Past Perfectwould have + V3Unreal past — couldn't happen now
Examples
  • Type 0: If you heat water to 100°C, it boils. ✓
  • Type 1: If he trains hard, he will pass the selection. ✓
  • Type 2: If I were the commanding officer, I would deploy the reserves. ✓
  • Type 3: If he had trained harder, he would have passed the test. ✓
⚑ Three Critical Conditional Traps
  • Never use "will" in the if-clause: ✗ "If he will come…" → ✓ "If he comes…"
  • Type 2 uses "were" not "was" for all persons: ✓ "If I were…", "If she were…" (subjunctive)
  • Type 3 uses "would have + V3" not "would + V3": ✗ "…he would pass" → ✓ "…he would have passed"
💬
Reported Speech — Tense Back-Shift
When reporting verb is past, all tenses shift back one step
▾

When converting direct speech to reported (indirect) speech, if the reporting verb (said, told, asked) is in the past tense, all tenses in the reported clause shift one step back into the past.

Direct Speech TenseReported Speech Tense
Simple Present → "I work hard."Simple Past → He said he worked hard.
Present Continuous → "I am working."Past Continuous → He said he was working.
Present Perfect → "I have finished."Past Perfect → He said he had finished.
Simple Past → "I left early."Past Perfect → He said he had left early.
will → "I will come."would → He said he would come.
can → "I can do it."could → He said he could do it.
may → "It may rain."might → He said it might rain.
⚑ When Back-Shift is NOT Required
  • Universal truths / scientific facts: "She said the sun rises in the east." (no shift) ✓
  • Habitual facts still true: "He said he gets up at 5 a.m." (habit still current — no shift needed)
  • Modal verbs that don't shift: would, could, should, might, ought to — already in past form, no further shift
Section 4 — Common High-Frequency Traps
🚨
Time Clauses — Never Use Future Tense
After when, before, after, until, as soon as, once, by the time → use Present
▾

In time clauses referring to the future, English uses the Present tense (not future) in the subordinate clause, even though the meaning is future.

The Rule

Time conjunctions: when, before, after, until/till, as soon as, once, by the time, whenever
In a future context → use Simple Present in the time clause, will in the main clause.

"I will contact you when I reach the base." ✓ (not "will reach")
"He will brief us before he leaves." ✓ (not "will leave")

Errors
  • I will call you when I will arrive. → when I arrive ✓
  • Wait here until he will come back. → until he comes back ✓
  • As soon as the operation will end, we will rest. → …operation ends ✓

Present Perfect vs Past Perfect — "Since" Trap & More
High-frequency patterns that look similar but are different
▾
PatternCorrect TenseExample
It is + time + sinceSimple PastIt is two years since he retired. ✓
It has been + time + sinceSimple PastIt has been years since we met. ✓
This is the first time…Present PerfectThis is the first time I have visited the base. ✓
That was the first time…Past PerfectThat was the first time he had flown. ✓
Hardly / Scarcely…whenPast Perf. → Simple PastHardly had he arrived when the alarm rang. ✓
No sooner…thanPast Perf. → Simple PastNo sooner had they landed than firing began. ✓
⚑ "Hardly/Scarcely…when" and "No sooner…than"
  • These are inverted structures: auxiliary verb comes before the subject
  • ✗ "No sooner they landed than firing began." → ✓ "No sooner had they landed than firing began."
  • ✗ "Hardly he arrived when…" → ✓ "Hardly had he arrived when…"
  • "No sooner…than" (not "when") | "Hardly/Scarcely…when" (not "than")
🔑
Present Perfect Continuous vs Present Perfect
Duration of an ongoing action vs completion — the aspect difference
▾

Both tenses use since/for — but they communicate different things.

TenseFocusExample
Present Perfect
(has/have + V3)		Completion — the action is doneHe has written three reports. (completed count)
Present Perfect Continuous
(has/have been + V-ing)		Duration — the action is still ongoingHe has been writing the report for two hours. (still writing)
Key Contrast
  • "She has lived in Delhi." → She lived there; may or may not still live there — focus on experience.
  • "She has been living in Delhi for five years." → She is still living there — focus on ongoing duration.
  • "He has been serving in the army for twenty years." → Still serving now ✓ (duration)
Common Error
  • He is serving in the army for ten years. → He has been serving in the army for ten years. ✓
  • She is working here since 2018. → She has been working here since 2018. ✓
Tenses — Complete Quick Reference
Simple Past vs Present Perfect
  • Simple Past → definite past time word present (yesterday, ago, last week, in 2010)
  • Present Perfect → no time word, or: just, already, yet, recently, ever, never
  • Since (point) + For (duration) → always Present Perfect
  • ✗ "I have seen him yesterday" → ✓ "I saw him yesterday"
  • ✗ "He left recently" → ✓ "He has left recently"
Stative Verbs — No Continuous Form
  • Mental: know, believe, understand, remember, think (opinion), mean
  • Emotion: love, hate, like, want, wish, need, prefer
  • Sense: see, hear, smell, taste, feel (passive)
  • Possession: have (=own), belong, contain, consist, include
  • Appearance: seem, appear, look (=seem), sound
  • ✗ "I am knowing" → ✓ "I know"
Conditionals — Fixed Tense Pairs
  • Type 0: If + Pres. Simple → Pres. Simple (facts)
  • Type 1: If + Pres. Simple → will + V1 (real future)
  • Type 2: If + Past Simple → would + V1 (hypothetical)
  • Type 3: If + Past Perfect → would have + V3 (unreal past)
  • ✗ Never "will" in if-clause | Type 2 uses "were" for all persons
Time Clauses & Future Tense
  • After when, before, after, until, as soon as, once → use Present (not future)
  • ✗ "when I will arrive" → ✓ "when I arrive"
  • No sooner…than → Past Perfect + Simple Past (inverted)
  • Hardly/Scarcely…when → Past Perfect + Simple Past (inverted)
  • "No sooner had he left than…" | "Hardly had he left when…"
Reported Speech Back-Shift
  • Present Simple → Past Simple
  • Present Continuous → Past Continuous
  • Present Perfect → Past Perfect
  • Simple Past → Past Perfect
  • will → would | can → could | may → might
  • No shift for: universal truths, would/could/should/might/ought to
Special Patterns to Memorise
  • "It is/has been + time + since" → Simple Past in "since" clause
  • "This is the first time…" → Present Perfect
  • "That was the first time…" → Past Perfect
  • "He has been serving for X years" — not "is serving" ✗
  • Past Perfect Continuous = had been + V-ing (duration before a past point)
  • Type 2 conditional: "If I were…" — never "If I was…" in formal English
